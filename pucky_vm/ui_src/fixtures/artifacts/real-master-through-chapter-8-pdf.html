Prologue The Phone Before the Phone On the morning of January 9, 2007, the phone had not yet become obvious. That is the strange thing to remember now. It is difficult to recover the old uncertainty, because the device that came afterward has made its own arrival seem inevitable. Once a thing has reorganized daily life, it is hard to imagine the world that did not yet know how badly it wanted it. But at the beginning of that January day in San Francisco, a phone was still, in most people's minds, a phone. It might have had a camera. It might have had a color screen. It might have flipped open with a satisfying snap or slid upward to reveal a little keyboard. It might have held a few songs, if the carrier and handset maker had cooperated long enough to let music onto it. It might have sent text messages. It might have opened a web page, slowly, awkwardly, through a carrier portal or a miniature browser that made the internet feel as though it had been folded until it cracked. It might have been a BlackBerry, in which case it did one thing so well that its owners would forgive almost everything else: it brought email to the hand, instantly, insistently, with the small percussive rhythm of thumbs on plastic keys. But a phone was not yet the surface on which much of life happened. It was not yet the universal remote for work, memory, money, friendship, maps, music, photography, shopping, dating, taxis, schools, restaurants, homes, and politics. It was not yet the object people patted their pockets to find, not because they might need to make a call, but because losing it meant losing the day. At Macworld that morning, Apple described its new device as three products in one: a mobile phone, a widescreen iPod with touch controls, and an internet communications device with desktop-class email, web browsing, search, and maps. The device had a large multi-touch display, worked with fingers rather than a stylus, and was announced at $499 for the 4GB model and $599 for the 8GB model. It would be sold through Apple and Cingular in the United States. Those facts now sound like museum labels. Four gigabytes. EDGE. Cingular. A two- megapixel camera. A phone that required iTunes. But at the time they sat inside a different theater of expectations. The stage was not only a physical stage. It was a stage built by decades of frustration. Every company that had tried to put computing into a pocket had left something behind. Some left ideas. Some left scars. Some left patents, interface metaphors, habits, failures,

Page 3

jokes, lawsuits, supply chains, and abandoned developer tools. Some left the memory of a thing that almost worked. Apple's own press release framed the iPhone as a new user interface based on a large multi-touch display, letting users control iPhone with just their fingers. Steve Jobs supplied the line that would echo through launch coverage, calling it revolutionary and magical. But if the device felt magical, the magic had a lineage. A spell, after all, is a sequence. It has ingredients. Inside that first iPhone were ghosts. There was the ghost of the personal digital assistant: the dream of carrying a calendar, contacts, notes, and to-dos in a pocket. There was the ghost of the pager: the small device that made people reachable before reachability became a default condition of modern work. There was the ghost of the car phone and the brick phone: expensive mobility as status, mobility as power, mobility as proof that your time mattered enough to interrupt. There was the ghost of the iPod: the pocket object as fashion, ritual, and identity. There was the ghost of the browser: the internet, shrunk down, resisted by tiny screens, slow networks, bad keyboards, and carrier business models. There was the ghost of the BlackBerry: the pocket as inbox. There was the ghost of the Newton: handwriting, ambition, ridicule, and the painful lesson that a future can be visible before it is usable. There was the ghost of General Magic: the networked personal communicator imagined with breathtaking clarity and built into a world that could not yet support it. The iPhone did not invent the desire to carry a computer. It did not invent mobile communication. It did not invent the idea of a touchscreen phone, the mobile browser, the portable media library, the electronic organizer, the camera phone, the app, or the wireless data network. What it did was gather many partial futures into one object at a moment when the surrounding world had finally become less hostile to the idea. That distinction matters because winner's history is too clean. It turns old failures into prefaces and old competitors into fools. But most of the people who failed on the road to the smartphone were not stupid. They were often early. Sometimes too early. Sometimes too faithful to the wrong customer. Sometimes trapped by the very thing they did best. Sometimes betrayed by batteries, screens, networks, chips, carriers, or prices. Sometimes they built something brilliant that ordinary people had no reason to buy. Sometimes they built something ordinary people did want, but the distribution system would not let them have it in the right way. The smartphone was not born in a single keynote. It was assembled over decades from separate answers to a question that kept changing shape: What should a pocket computer be?

At first, the answer was not "a phone." It was a calendar. A contacts list. A calculator. A diary. A pager. A radio telephone. A little electronic secretary. A notebook that did not run out of paper. A business machine for travelers. A way for a doctor to be summoned. A way for a stockbroker to call a client from a car. A way for a teenager to type "where r u" under a desk. A way for a lawyer to feel the pulse of an office while standing in an airport. A way for music to leave the stereo and enter the street. Long before people carried app stores in their pockets, they carried pieces of themselves. A name and number written on paper. A beeper number clipped to a belt. A folded map. A wallet. Keys. A cassette player. A camera for vacations. A calendar in a briefcase. A phone card. A pack of coins for the payphone. A pocket calculator. A notepad. A pen. The smartphone would eventually eat all of these. But before it ate them, it had to become believable. The first chapter of this story begins in a world where portability was not yet smallness. Portable often meant "can be moved if necessary." A portable computer might weigh as much as luggage. A mobile phone might be attached to a car. A wireless message might not contain a message at all, only a beep and a number to call back. The future was already trying to move, but it still needed a handle, a trunk, a belt clip, a briefcase, a docking cradle, or a wall charger. The object in your hand began as a series of compromises too big for the hand. Part I Dreams of a Pocket Computer Chapter 1 The Portable Future Before It Fit in a Pocket The first thing to understand about the smartphone is that it arrived after a long apprenticeship in inconvenience. Before the phone became small enough to disappear into a pocket, mobility was noisy, heavy, expensive, and strangely public. To be mobile was not yet to be casual. It was to be equipped. A person who wanted to be reachable in the early mobile age did not simply carry a rectangle of glass and metal. He might carry a pager. He might install a radio telephone in a car. He might own an electronic organizer with a tiny screen and rubber keys. He might keep

Page 5

a paper diary, a Rolodex, and a calculator. He might carry a laptop that stretched the definition of "lap." He might know the locations of payphones in airports and hotels. He might have a phone number at home, a phone number at work, a fax number, a pager number, and an assistant who knew which one mattered at which hour. The pre-smartphone world was not disconnected. It was connected through rituals. You called places, not people. You left messages with receptionists. You checked answering machines. You looked up numbers in books. You wrote appointments by hand. You carried scraps of paper. You memorized directions or printed them. You waited. Waiting was part of the system. Then little devices began to attack waiting. The pager was one of the earliest. It did not look like the future in the way later devices would. It did not offer a glowing window into the world. It did not promise creativity or play. It was a command object. It beeped, and someone somewhere had decided that you needed to respond. For decades, pagers belonged first to professions where availability had obvious value: doctors, emergency workers, technicians, managers, people whose absence could become a problem. In the 1960s, Motorola's Pageboy was already being described as compact by the standards of its day, a small receiver worn by people who needed to be summoned rather than entertained. A 1967 Time item placed Pageboy pricing at $180 for low-frequency models and $275 for VHF models, serious money for a device whose main function was interruption. The genius of the pager was also its cruelty. It separated notification from communication. It could tell you that someone wanted you, but not always why. Early one- way pagers did not give you a conversation. Some did not even give you text. They gave you a signal, a number, a demand: find a phone. That demand built a habit. A person heard or felt the alert, looked down, and reorganized the moment around a remote claim on attention. The modern notification economy would later become more colorful, more personalized, more addictive, and more lucrative. But the essential choreography was there: a small object close to the body, an incoming signal, a break in whatever was happening, and a new priority. A pager did not need to be loved. It needed to be obeyed. The mobile phone began with a different kind of authority. It was not merely an interruption device. It was a declaration. In the 1970s and 1980s, a mobile telephone said something about its owner before a call was ever placed. It said that this person's time was valuable, that business could not wait, that the office had extended into the car.

The mobile telephone was not born pocketable. It came through cars and trunks and antennas and systems built with enormous patience. Bell Labs had conceptualized cellular communications in 1947, and the U.S. cellular system that became AMPS - Advanced Mobile Phone System - emerged from years of work by Bell Labs, Motorola, regulators, and carriers. AMPS was officially introduced in the Americas on October 13, 1983, and became the principal analog mobile phone system in North America through the 1980s and beyond. The word "cellular" described a network architecture, not a lifestyle. The system divided geography into cells so frequencies could be reused. That idea would eventually support mass mobile communication, but the first public experience of cellular service was not mass. It was scarce. It was expensive. It was wrapped in the language of business necessity. The early mobile phone had to fight the automobile for independence. Before handheld cellular phones, mobile telephone equipment was often installed in vehicles. Wired later summarized the pre-handheld world bluntly: to make a mobile call in those days, one might need thousands of dollars, around thirty pounds of equipment in a trunk, and a special antenna. Mobility existed, but it came with a machine that had wheels. That is why the first handheld cellular call in 1973 became such a useful origin story. Martin Cooper of Motorola walked through New York with a prototype and called a rival at Bell Labs. The story is almost too neat: the portable upstart calling the infrastructure incumbent. The prototype was not pocketable in any modern sense. Wired describes the DynaTAC prototype as roughly nine inches tall, two and a half pounds, packed with thirty circuit boards, with about thirty-five minutes of talk time and a ten-hour recharge. But it did not live in the trunk. That was the point. A decade later, the commercial version still felt like a brick because it was one. Motorola's DynaTAC 8000X, introduced commercially in the early 1980s, became the emblem of the first handheld cellular age: tall, heavy, expensive, and miraculous. The device had a small numerical display, a physical keypad, and the aura of something smuggled from the future before the future had made room for it. Contemporary accounts and later histories generally place its retail price in the thousands of dollars; Wired's retrospective gives a first-adopter price of $3,500, while many device histories cite $3,995 for the 1984 commercial product. The important thing for the story is not which number one chooses down to the dollar. It is that the number belonged to a different class of object. This was not a mass-market appliance. It was a mobile status machine. Imagine the physical act of using it. You did not glance at it. You wielded it. You raised a long beige object with a rubber antenna to your ear. You made calls that had to justify their cost. You watched the battery. You carried the charger as part of the device's reality. The phone call was still the center of

the experience because there was not much else for the device to do. Its memory might hold numbers, but not a life. Its screen might show digits, but not a map, a face, a message thread, or a photograph. And yet, in that limited machine, two future ideas were already alive. First, the phone number was becoming mobile. It no longer had to point only to a place. Second, reachability was becoming personal. Not universal, not cheap, not normal - not yet - but personal. The early mobile phone did not democratize communication. It privatized priority. That is one reason the car phone became a cultural symbol before the handheld became a mass object. It belonged naturally to people already accustomed to being served by infrastructure: executives, salespeople, financiers, lawyers, agents, politicians, doctors. The car itself became an office annex. Calls happened between meetings, between airports, between buildings. A person in motion could still transact. This mattered. The smartphone would later be sold as a consumer device, an entertainment device, a social device, a camera, a music player, a playground. But its earliest mobile ancestors were built around work. The first question was not "What can I do with this?" It was "Who can reach me, and what can I do before I arrive?" That business logic shaped the devices. Early mobility privileged urgency over intimacy. It prized connection, not delight. It served people who could attach a price to availability. The pocket computer, meanwhile, grew from a different anxiety: not the fear of being unreachable, but the fear of forgetting. The paper organizer had already solved part of the problem. Long before PDAs, people carried calendars, address books, notebooks, and elaborate personal planning systems. The Filofax and its rivals were not primitive just because they were paper. They were flexible, durable, instantly readable, and socially legible. They worked without batteries. They could hold business cards, receipts, passwords, sketches, and private notes. They could be flipped through in a meeting without looking like a toy. The challenge for electronic organizers was therefore not merely to be more futuristic. They had to be better than paper at enough things to justify their weaknesses. Early pocket computers and organizers tried to answer that challenge in different ways. Some looked like calculators with ambition. Some looked like tiny typewriters. Some were programmable. Some were essentially address books with screens. Some were fascinating to hobbyists and baffling to everyone else.

The Sharp PC-1211, introduced around 1980 and sold in a Radio Shack version as the TRS-80 Pocket Computer, was part of this world of pocketable computing before the PDA category had hardened. It had a small LCD, a QWERTY-style keyboard, and BASIC programmability. It could be a calculator, a programmable tool, a small computer in the literal sense. But it was not yet the kind of thing an ordinary person would recognize as a daily companion. That distinction is important. A machine can be pocket-sized without being personal. A pocket computer that asks its user to program is still, in a sense, a computer. A personal digital assistant asks a different question: What if the machine knew the small recurring facts of your life? Names. Phone numbers. Meetings. Notes. Reminders. The scattered administrative burden of being a person. In Britain, Psion became one of the important early answers. The Psion Organiser, launched in 1984, was a small pocket computer with a hard sliding cover, a compact keypad, a one-line display in its first version, and removable storage modules. Later histories often call it the first practical electronic organizer or personal digital assistant because it brought together a diary, database-like storage, calculator functions, and portability in a way that people could actually use. The Psion Organiser did not look like a modern smartphone ancestor if one expects the ancestor to look like a sheet of glass. It looked more like a thick calculator that had learned about appointments. Its keyboard was not inviting in the way later keyboards would be. Its display did not create a world. But it had one of the most important qualities in the history of pocket devices: it respected the pocket. The pocket is a brutal design judge. A desk tolerates clutter. A briefcase tolerates bulk. A car tolerates equipment. A pocket does not. A thing in a pocket must survive being forgotten, sat on, dropped, ignored, grabbed, and used in bad light. It must not demand ceremony. It must earn its place among keys and coins. The Psion Organiser and similar devices hinted at a future where the computer was not an event. It was just there. But the device that most clearly advertised the ambition of the electronic assistant was still to come. Apple's Newton would take the idea of a digital companion and give it a name that stuck to an entire category: personal digital assistant. John Sculley, Apple's chief executive, is widely associated with popularizing or coining that term in connection with Newton; Wired later noted that Sculley used "Personal Digital Assistant" to refer to the Newton and had described a related "Knowledge Navigator" vision in his 1987 book Odyssey.

The phrase mattered because it was not "pocket computer." A computer was a machine. An assistant was a relationship. This is where the smartphone's ancestry becomes psychologically interesting. The industry kept changing the noun. Phone. Organizer. Communicator. Assistant. Companion. Pager. Terminal. Computer. Media player. Platform. Each noun carried a theory of use. Each implied a different buyer, a different interface, a different price, and a different standard of success. Call something a computer, and people expect power. Call it a phone, and they expect reliability. Call it an organizer, and they expect order. Call it an assistant, and they expect intelligence. Call it a communicator, and they expect other people to be there. Much of the next twenty years would be a fight over which noun mattered most. The tyranny of small screens The oldest problem in mobile computing was not that engineers lacked imagination. It was that the physical world kept saying no. Screens were small because big screens consumed power, cost money, broke easily, and made devices too large. Batteries were weak because chemistry was stubborn. Processors had to sip power. Memory was expensive. Wireless networks were slow, sparse, or nonexistent. Input was a nightmare. A full keyboard made a device wider. A phone keypad made text painful. A stylus could be precise but easy to lose and awkward in motion. Handwriting recognition promised natural input, but natural for the user did not mean easy for the machine. Every early pocket device was a treaty among these constraints. A pager could be tiny because it did almost nothing. A mobile phone could be portable because it gave up most of computing. An electronic organizer could last because it did not maintain a constant network connection. A laptop could compute because it lived in a bag and consumed a bigger battery. A car phone could transmit because the car carried the mass. The smartphone would eventually appear to dissolve these tradeoffs. That was part of its magic. But it did not dissolve them. It inherited them and hid them behind integration. In the early years, the compromises sat on the surface.

Consider the act of entering text. Text would become central to mobile life: email, search, messaging, notes, usernames, passwords, captions, commands. But text was also the enemy of small devices. A telephone keypad was designed for numbers. T9 and multi-tap systems later made alphabetic entry possible, even fast for skilled users, but there was always a translation happening between the user's language and the hardware's original purpose. Small QWERTY keyboards solved one problem and created others: thumb typing was faster, but keys took space, trapped crumbs, broke, and forced the screen to share the face of the device. Stylus input let a screen become the interface, but then the user had to remove a pen, aim carefully, and accept that the device was now a two-handed object. The dream of handwriting recognition came from an obvious intuition: people already knew how to write. Why not let them write on the screen? But handwriting is personal, messy, inconsistent, slanted, hurried, and full of context. Humans read it with forgiveness. Machines did not. This was the trap that would later catch Newton. But before Newton became a punchline, handwriting recognition was a deeply plausible dream. It promised to make computing less computer-like. It promised a device that could enter the human world rather than forcing humans into keyboard discipline. The early portable future was filled with promises like that. Many were not wrong. They were only incomplete. Mobility before the internet It is tempting to assume that mobile computing was waiting for the internet. In one sense, it was. The smartphone would become the internet's most intimate machine. But in another sense, the desire for mobile computing preceded the consumer internet because the first portable problems were older than the web. People needed to know where to be. They needed to know whom to call. They needed to remember what had been promised. They needed to calculate. They needed to send and receive urgent information. They needed to preserve status while in motion.

A doctor did not need a web browser to need a pager. A salesperson did not need Google Maps to need a car phone. A manager did not need an app store to want a portable calendar. A traveler did not need social media to want an address book that did not vanish when paper was misplaced. The internet later gave these devices a vast new reason to exist, but it did not create the human needs underneath them. It amplified them, connected them, monetized them, and made them continuous. Before continuity, there were sessions. You sat down at a computer to compute. You went to a phone to call. You opened an organizer to plan. You checked a pager to see whether someone wanted you. You turned on a laptop when the conditions were right: a flat surface, a charged battery, perhaps a modem, perhaps a phone line. Each device had a moment. Each moment had a beginning and an end. The smartphone would eventually erase those boundaries. But in the pre-smartphone world, boundaries were how life stayed manageable. Reachability was partial. Information was partial. Mobility was partial. This partialness shaped expectations. A device did not have to do everything. In fact, doing too much could make it suspect. Early users accepted narrowness when the narrow function mattered enough. The pager did not apologize for not being a phone. The car phone did not apologize for not being a computer. The organizer did not apologize for not being entertaining. Later, convergence would become the prize. But before convergence, specialization won. The car, the belt, the briefcase, the pocket Every mobile device has a natural home on the body or near it. That home determines its character. The car phone belonged to the car. It extended the office into the road. It made motion productive, but only a certain kind of motion: motion with a vehicle, a subscription, and enough money to justify the system. The pager belonged to the belt. It attached itself to the body like an obligation. Its position mattered. On a belt or waistband, it was visible enough to signal role. Doctors, technicians, and later teenagers wore pagers in ways that made the device part of identity. The laptop belonged to the briefcase. It promised mobile work, but with a ritual: open the case, find power if possible, unfold the screen, wait. Even the term "portable" often meant transportable rather than comfortably handheld.

The organizer belonged to the pocket or bag. It was closest to the later smartphone in intimacy. It did not merely connect the user to others. It remembered things on the user's behalf. The smartphone's eventual power came from occupying all four spaces. It could be a car device, a belt device, a briefcase device, and a pocket device. It could navigate, interrupt, compute, remember, entertain, transact, photograph, and call. But the road there passed through devices that were much less ambitious because ambition was dangerous. Ambition increased cost. Ambition increased battery drain. Ambition increased complexity. Ambition increased the number of ways a device could disappoint. The pre-smartphone industry learned this lesson repeatedly, often at great expense. Simon, or the future with a short battery If one wanted to choose an early device that looked, in retrospect, like a smartphone before the word had settled, IBM and BellSouth's Simon Personal Communicator would be a strong candidate. Simon went on sale in August 1994. It had a touchscreen, email capability, fax capability, a calendar, an address book, a calculator, a notepad, a sketch pad, and other built-in functions. It could be navigated with a stylus. It cost about $1,100, or about $900 with a two- year BellSouth contract, and later fell to $600 with a contract. It lasted only about six months on the market and sold roughly 50,000 units, according to later retrospectives. This is the kind of product that makes historians reach for the phrase "ahead of its time." But that phrase can be lazy. Simon was not merely ahead of its time. It was inside its time, trapped by it. The device had no web browser. That sounds damning only if one forgets that consumer web culture was just beginning. Its email was not the effortless cloud inbox later users would expect; Time's retrospective notes that Simon used Lotus cc:Mail and depended on a computer running the mail software as a "post office." A user could send faxes, which made perfect sense in the business world of the mid-1990s. A user could touch a screen, but not with the fluidity that later capacitive multi-touch would normalize. A user could carry a combined phone and PDA, but carrying it meant accepting bulk and battery limits. Simon is valuable because it shows how many pieces of the smartphone existed before the system around them was ready.

Touchscreen: yes. Apps, though not called apps: yes. Email: yes. Calendar and contacts: yes. Wireless phone: yes. Mass-market desirability: no. A device can contain the future and still fail to become it. Simon also shows why the phrase "first smartphone" is less useful than it seems. First according to which definition? A phone with PDA functions? A cellular device with a touchscreen? A device with email? A general-purpose mobile computer? A platform with third-party applications? A handheld internet terminal? Definitions change because the category changed. In 1994, Simon's problem was not that people had no use for its functions. Businesspeople did use phones, calendars, fax, contacts, and email. The problem was that the bundle was expensive, compromised, and early. The surrounding habits were not yet there. The network was not yet there. The battery was not yet there. The screen was not yet there. The emotional language was not yet there. Simon was a product for a world that still thought of mobile computing as work. The later smartphone would make work only one mode among many. The grammar of interruption While companies were trying to put calendars and contacts into electronic organizers, pagers were teaching society a different lesson: interruption could be portable. This lesson moved from profession to culture. In medicine and emergency services, the pager had a clear logic. Someone needed to be reached because a situation demanded response. But as paging spread, the meaning changed. By the 1980s and 1990s, pagers became associated not only with doctors and technicians but also with teenagers, drug dealers in popular imagination, office workers, and anyone who wanted a cheap way to receive a signal away from home. The pager did not need a voice plan. It did not require a long call. It could be cheaper than a mobile phone and more portable than a car telephone. Numeric pagers turned phone numbers into messages. People developed codes. A callback number could carry urgency,

Page 14

affection, secrecy, irritation. Later alphanumeric pagers made the device more message-like. Two-way pagers pushed still closer to the territory that BlackBerry would later own. Here, too, the smartphone inherited a behavior before it inherited a technology. The habit was not simply communication. It was checking. Look down. Who wants me? What happened? What do I need to do now? Every time someone checked a pager, the pocket became a site of possible news. The modern smartphone would multiply that sensation until it became ambient. But the pager's simplicity makes the pattern visible. There was no feed, no infinite scroll, no camera roll, no inbox full of promotional mail, no social graph. There was just a signal. And even that was enough to alter a moment. The pager trained people to carry interruption close to the body. The organizer as private infrastructure If the pager externalized urgency, the organizer internalized order. A paper organizer could hold the private architecture of a life: appointments, phone numbers, birthdays, project notes, passwords, expenses, sketches, plans. Losing it was not like losing a notebook. It could feel like losing memory. Electronic organizers promised a new kind of control. They could search. They could alarm. They could calculate. They could store names without rewriting pages. They could, eventually, sync. That last word would become one of the great transitional concepts in mobile history. Syncing meant the pocket device was not yet fully independent. It was a satellite. The main computer still lived on the desk. The pocket device carried a copy of the essential facts. It was not the center of digital life. It was a dependent companion. This dependency shaped early PDA culture. A device like a Palm Pilot - still ahead in our story - would later succeed partly because it accepted the desktop as the source of truth. It did not try to be a full computer. It tried to be a fast, pocketable extension of one. That was a humbler ambition than Newton's, and in many ways a smarter one. But before Palm, before the HotSync cradle became a little ritual of digital order, the industry had to figure out what kind of data belonged in the pocket. Contacts obviously belonged there. Phone numbers were mobile before phones were. A person needed numbers wherever a phone could be found.

Page 15

Calendars belonged there. Time is the original mobile operating system. It structures where the body must go. Notes belonged there, but notes were harder. A note could be anything: a grocery list, a quote, a meeting record, a sketch, an idea. Capturing notes required input, and input was the weak point of almost every small machine. Tasks belonged there. But task lists revealed a subtle truth: a digital assistant that reminded you of obligations could become another source of obligation. The organizer was not only a convenience. It was a discipline device. That is one reason people loved and hated electronic organizers. The promise was control. The risk was that control required maintenance. Every address had to be entered. Every appointment had to be typed or written. Every category had to be chosen. Every battery had to be watched. A paper notebook could be messy and still useful. A digital organizer often demanded structure before it gave structure back. The smartphone would later reduce some of this friction through cloud syncing, imported contacts, email integration, calendars, search, and defaults. But in the early era, the user often had to become the system administrator of their own pocket. The portable computer that was not yet personal One reason the PDA felt exciting was that laptops, for all their power, were not intimate. Portable computers had been shrinking through the 1980s, but many still belonged more naturally to luggage than to clothing. Some early machines were "portable" because a human could carry them from one outlet to another. The Osborne 1, the Compaq Portable, and other luggable systems were important steps in mobile computing, but they did not satisfy the fantasy of a device that stayed with the person all day. Even as laptops improved, they preserved the session. Open, boot, work, close. The smartphone would later make computing continuous. The early portable computer made computing movable. That difference mattered for design. If a device is used in sessions, it can tolerate setup. If a device is used in moments, setup kills it. A pocket device has to wake quickly. It has to answer a small question before the user forgets the question. It has to support glances, not only sessions. The pager was excellent at this. The electronic organizer tried. The early mobile phone was good for one task but poor at everything else. The laptop was powerful but slow to enter the moment.

The smartphone would win because it became a moment machine. But that was not yet clear. In the 1980s and early 1990s, computing still seemed anchored to desks, even when the desk moved. The personal computer revolution had put computation in homes and offices. The next question was whether computation could become bodily. This is where science fiction, corporate prototypes, and product roadmaps began to blur. The idea of the "personal communicator" appeared again and again because it named a wish larger than any shipping product. People imagined a small device that could help them organize life, communicate, and access information. General Magic's 1990 mission statement, later quoted in Wired, dreamed of "small, intimate life support systems" that people would carry everywhere, helping them organize, communicate, and access information. The language is striking because it sounds less like a gadget pitch than a description of the smartphone's eventual role. But General Magic's devices were not yet in this chapter's main story. They belong to the next phase, when the dream became explicit and the failure became instructive. For now, what matters is that the dream was already in the air. The pocket computer was not simply a smaller computer. It was imagined as a life- support system. That phrase would have sounded grandiose in 1990. It sounds less grandiose now. Price as destiny No history of mobile devices makes sense without price. A technology can be possible and still irrelevant to most people. The early mobile phone was technically astonishing but economically exclusive. Pagers were cheaper and therefore spread differently. Electronic organizers had to compete not only with other electronics but with paper, which was cheap, flexible, and already understood. Price determined not only who could buy a device but what kind of device people believed it was. A $3,500 or $3,995 handheld phone was a business tool, a luxury object, or a status symbol. A $199 pocket computer was closer to a hobbyist or productivity purchase. A $700 PDA was an ambitious executive gadget. A subsidized $900 IBM Simon was still a serious business expense. These numbers shaped expectations. If a device cost as much as a computer, users expected computer-like power. If it cost as much as an accessory, users tolerated limitations. The emotional problem of the pocket A pocket device has to do more than function. It has to be tolerated. This is easy to overlook. People will put up with an ugly printer in an office or a loud refrigerator in a kitchen. But a device carried close to the body enters a more intimate zone. It is touched constantly. It is seen by others. It adds weight. It creates anxiety if forgotten. It can embarrass, empower, distract, or comfort. A messaging device could not be mostly reliable. As devices converged, the standards of each category collided. The phone brought expectations of availability. The computer brought expectations of flexibility. The organizer brought expectations of memory. The pager brought expectations of urgency.

The future as prototype By the early 1990s, the ingredients were scattered everywhere. There were mobile networks, but they were slow, analog, expensive, and uneven. Chapter 2 Newton and the Perils of Ambition The pocket was the first problem. Before the handwriting recognition, before the jokes, before the comic-strip humiliation, before the loyal cult of users, before Steve Jobs returned to Apple and cut the Newton loose, there was a simpler, more physical question: could this thing fit in John Sculley's pocket? The requirement sounds almost comic now, but it carried the whole burden of the category. If the device did not fit, it was not really personal. It might be portable, but portable was not enough. Portable computers already existed. Laptops existed. Luggables existed. Calculators existed. Apple wanted something stranger: a computer that could travel not in a bag, not on a desk, not in a car, but with the body. The story, as later told by people who worked on it, began with a pitch. Michael Tchao, an Apple product executive, presented the idea to John Sculley in the early 1990s. Wired later described the pitch as happening on an airplane in early 1991. Apple would announce the Newton the following year, and the first MessagePad would go on sale in August 1993. It was intended not merely as a product, but as a new category of handheld computing: a platform for devices that could slip into pockets and leave the office behind. That distinction mattered to Apple. The company did not want merely to shrink the Macintosh. The Newton team was trying to make something with its own architecture, its own interface, its own way of storing information, its own programming language, its own assumptions about how a person might interact with a computer while standing, walking, waiting, talking, or moving from one room to another. A desktop computer met you at a desk. The Newton wanted to meet you in motion. In the late 1980s and early 1990s, that was an almost reckless ambition. Screens were small and gray. Batteries were weak. Processors were slow. Memory was expensive. Wireless communications were limited. The public internet was not yet a mass consumer habit. The world had not yet learned to expect a computer in every hand. Apple was not trying to ride a mature wave. It was trying to name one. Sculley supplied the name that would cling to the device and the category around it: personal digital assistant. PDA. The phrase sounded futuristic without sounding technical. It avoided the coldness of "pocket computer." It suggested not a machine, but a helper. A secretary. A memory aid. A small intelligence. The term itself did a lot of work. It implied that the object would understand you, not merely obey you.

Page 23

That was the promise. It was also the trap. A computer could make demands of its user. It could require a keyboard, files, menus, disks, manuals, patience. An assistant was judged by a different standard. An assistant should know what you mean. Newton would be punished for the difference. The black object The Newton did not look like a shrunken Macintosh. That was part of its drama. Apple in the 1980s had been identified with a particular visual language: beige machines, orderly lines, the disciplined friendliness of the Macintosh era. Newton was darker, flatter, more secretive. The design team explored what later accounts called the "Batman concept": black, sculpted, sleek, unlike the old Snow White industrial design language that had defined much of Apple's previous hardware. The team was trying to build a portable object that felt new not only in function but in posture. It was not a tiny office machine. It was meant to look like something that belonged to the hand. But the hand is not a forgiving place to design for. A desktop computer can be wide. A laptop can unfold. A phone can be long and narrow if its job is mostly calling. A personal digital assistant has to invite grasping, reading, pointing, writing, and carrying. It has to be large enough to display words, forms, calendars, and notes, but small enough to avoid becoming ridiculous. Its shape must fit not only the hand but the owner's imagination of being the sort of person who carries it. Gavin Ivester, who worked on Newton industrial design, later recalled that width mattered because it affected how a person held the device. The team needed a form that could be reached around securely with fingers and thumb. In another recollection, the designers joked about sneaking into Sculley's house and sewing bigger pockets into his clothes. They made the device fit, but just barely, and "fit" remained more conceptual than effortless. The first MessagePad was pocketable in the way a hardcover book is pocketable if the pocket is generous and the wearer is committed. It was not a featherweight object. It was not something one forgot was there. It was a carried thing, a small slab of ambition, closer to a notepad than a phone. Still, to see it in 1993 was to see a serious attempt at a different future. The screen faced upward. The stylus came out. The user could write directly on the surface. Applications were not merely icons leading to separate little worlds; information

Page 24

could flow through the device in ways that anticipated later mobile thinking. The Newton could take notes, store contacts, manage calendars, and even send a fax. Apple wanted the device to be not just a digital notebook but an information appliance that could recognize intention. That last idea was the dangerous one. The Newton was not sold as a box that stored data. It was sold as a device that understood. It would recognize your handwriting. It would help organize your life. It would turn rough human input into structured digital order. For a company famous for making computers feel humane, it was a natural dream. For a product launching in 1993, it was a brutal promise to keep. The assistant who had to read Handwriting recognition was not a decorative feature. It was the center of the Newton's public meaning. The idea was obvious enough to feel inevitable. People already wrote in notebooks. They already made grocery lists, meeting notes, appointment reminders, phone numbers, sketches, arrows, boxes, and names. A keyboard on a pocket device was awkward. A full-size keyboard made the device too large. A miniature keyboard demanded training and patience. A pen was familiar. A pen was personal. A pen was what people already used when they wanted to capture thought quickly. So the Newton invited the user to write. This was supposed to remove friction. Instead, it created a new form of intimacy: the machine looked at your handwriting and judged it. The problem was not simply technical. It was emotional. When a keyboard produces the wrong letter, the user usually blames the finger. When handwriting recognition produces the wrong word, the user may blame the machine, or the hand, or the entire premise of the device. The failure feels less mechanical than personal. The machine has misunderstood you. The Newton team understood that handwriting recognition was essential. Steve Capps, who led much of the user interface and software work, later said the team was "way ahead of the technology" and that handwriting recognition was only barely functioning when the product started shipping. It was supposed to be the killer feature; instead, it became the feature that did the most damage. The early MessagePad did not merely fail in private. It failed in public, and at precisely the worst point: the place where Apple had promised magic.

A user could forgive many limitations if the device did the basic job. It was Apple. It did not get to be mediocre quietly. The jokes arrived quickly. The most famous came from Doonesbury. Garry Trudeau devoted a week of the strip to mocking the Newton's handwriting recognition. One gag became shorthand for the whole device: the machine mangling an ordinary phrase into nonsense. Wired later noted that Trudeau had not even tried the Newton when he made the joke; he was lampooning gadget culture and found in Newton's already-battered handwriting reputation an easy target. But the result was devastating. The joke fixed the Newton in public memory as a device that could not read. That is the cruelty of early failure. A product can improve, but its reputation may not. The Newton became the rare device whose flaw was legible even to people who never touched one. You did not need to understand processors, object stores, operating systems, or battery chemistry. You only needed to understand the embarrassment of a computer misreading handwriting. The joke explained itself. This is why Newton's failure became so culturally durable. Many failed gadgets disappear because ordinary people never learn enough about them to mock them. Newton failed in a way everyone could understand. It misunderstood words. A $700 notebook Price made the joke sharper. If the Newton had been a cheap toy, handwriting mistakes might have seemed amusing. If it had been a limited experimental device sold only to specialists, flaws might have been tolerated. But Apple was trying to open a new category. The Newton was positioned as a serious personal technology object, and serious personal technology objects were judged against the ordinary things they claimed to replace. A paper notebook did not cost $700. A pen did not need batteries. A calendar did not crash. An address book did not need to learn your handwriting.

The first Newton was widely remembered as launching around the $700 mark. That number mattered less as a price than as a challenge. A $700 digital assistant had to be much better than paper. Not futuristic in theory. Better in daily use. The Newton was not absurdly expensive compared with some earlier mobile technologies. A DynaTAC-class cellular phone could cost thousands of dollars. Laptops were often expensive. That was not a fair description of everything Newton could do, but it was a powerful one. A product's enemy is not always its direct competitor. Sometimes the enemy is the ordinary object that already works well enough. The Newton asked users to believe that the notebook should become intelligent. Many users were not yet convinced that the notebook needed electricity. What the Newton actually did The public memory of Newton is so dominated by handwriting that it can obscure what made the device interesting. A bad product does not usually produce a cult. A merely incompetent machine does not inspire people to keep using it decades later, run conferences around it, repair it, emulate it, collect it, and speak of it with a tenderness usually reserved for objects that once felt alive. The Newton attracted ridicule because it was flawed. It attracted devotion because it was also, in important ways, extraordinary. The device was trying to create a mobile information environment before the world had settled on how such a thing should behave. It was not simply a contact list and a calendar. It had an operating system designed around a different model of information. Newton OS stored data in structures often called "soups," which allowed different parts of the system to share information in ways that were unusual for the time. Notes, contacts, dates, and other pieces of information could be treated less like isolated files and more like living data. It was an approach better suited to a mobile assistant than to a desktop filing cabinet. The user's life did not arrive in neat application silos. Names appeared in notes.

Dates appeared in sentences. Phone numbers appeared in messages. The Newton tried to notice. That noticing was one of its most futuristic qualities. Wired's later account emphasized that the Newton could take a phrase like a lunch meeting with someone tomorrow at noon and turn it into a calendar item. That sounds modest only because later mobile devices made such behavior normal. In the early 1990s, it suggested a different relationship between person and machine. You were not simply entering data. You were telling the device something, and it might understand the shape of the instruction. This is the thread that connects Newton not only to later PDAs but to the broader dream of assistants, natural language interfaces, reminders, smart calendars, and eventually voice- driven mobile software. The Newton did not deliver that future cleanly, but it carried the intuition. It also understood shapes. Later Newton users remembered being able to draw a rough box or circle and have the device clean it up into a geometric shape. One longtime user recalled being fascinated by the clean interface, the sharp pixels, and the sense that there was nothing else quite like it unless one lived in a research lab. These details matter because they show the split between reputation and experience. The public remembered the Newton as the machine that could not read handwriting. Some users remembered it as a machine that did things no other consumer device did. That split appears again and again in smartphone history. A product can fail commercially and still carry ideas that outlive it. It can be wrong for the market and right about the direction. It can lose the year and still influence the decade. Newton was such a product. The architecture of a small future The Newton team could not simply put Macintosh software into a smaller case. The hardware would not allow it, and the use case did not want it. Battery life alone forced a different design. Capps later described battery constraints as requiring a new architecture. The machine needed to run on limited power, conserve memory, wake quickly, and perform enough computation to support handwriting recognition, calendars, notes, and communications without behaving like a desktop computer.

This led Apple toward a processor story that would become far bigger than the Newton itself. The Newton used an ARM processor. At the time, ARM was not yet the nearly invisible foundation of global mobile computing. Its rise was intertwined with the search for power- efficient processors that could make a handheld computer plausible. Apple, Acorn, and VLSI formed Advanced RISC Machines as a joint venture in 1990, and Apple's Newton project was one of the early reasons the architecture mattered commercially. Later histories of Arm point to the Newton as one of the early commercial devices that helped validate the approach. That fact gives the Newton an odd afterlife. The product failed, but one of its enabling technologies became central to the mobile world. ARM processors would become foundational to smartphones, tablets, wearables, and embedded devices because they offered a way to balance performance and power consumption. The Newton did not make ARM inevitable, but it was part of the lineage. In retrospect, this is one of the most important ways to understand failed products. They do not vanish as cleanly as sales charts imply. Their components, teams, suppliers, tools, ideas, and assumptions often move elsewhere. A device can fail as a market offering and still help train the supply chain for the next thing. Newton's architecture was not just technical. It was philosophical. It assumed the mobile computer needed its own logic. It could not be a desktop squeezed down to size. It had to be designed around low power, memory constraints, pen input, quick access, persistent personal data, and mobile use. That sounds obvious now because the smartphone made it obvious. The Newton team had to discover it while the category was still fog. Hype before readiness Newton had another burden: Apple told the world to expect too much. John Sculley was not timid about the category. In the early 1990s, Apple was trying to show that it could define the next phase of computing after the Macintosh. The company had reason to be anxious. Microsoft Windows was rising. Apple's product line was complicated. The company was searching for a new technological narrative. Newton offered one. The PDA could be Apple's next defining idea. But a new category needs careful expectation-setting. If the first product is rough, the launch story has to leave room for roughness. The Newton's launch did the opposite. It invited people to see a revolution.

The gap between promise and experience became part of the product. This was not unique to Apple. Many companies later made the same mistake. They announced a new category as though the announcement itself could solve the problems of price, timing, component maturity, user behavior, and distribution. A demo can make a device look inevitable for a few minutes. Daily use is less forgiving. Newton's daily use exposed all the weak points at once. The handwriting recognition was unreliable enough to become famous. The battery life of the earliest models disappointed users. Memory was limited. The device was expensive. The category was unfamiliar. The supporting ecosystem was incomplete. The software was ambitious but not mature. The physical object was portable but not effortless. The problem was not that Newton had no value. The problem was that its value required patience, and the launch had trained people to expect magic. When a product is sold as magic, normal defects become betrayals. The improved Newton nobody remembers The Newton did improve. This is one of the most important parts of the story, because it complicates the simple failure narrative. The early Newton deserved many of its criticisms, but the later Newton did not remain the same device. Apple and the Newton team kept working. They addressed battery life, memory, note-taking, and recognition. The platform got better. Newton's trust had been damaged early. The later devices became genuinely admired among certain users. By the time of the MessagePad 2000 and 2100, the platform had become far more capable. The devoted few The Newton inspired an unusual kind of loyalty. The loyalists did not merely remember it fondly. They continued to use it, repair it, adapt it, and gather around it. The NewtonTalk mailing list, the Worldwide Newton Conference, emulator projects, software patches, hardware hacks, and private collections all became part of the device's afterlife. That last point matters. The Newton's limitations helped define its charm for later users. It was not trying to be a portal into everything. The Palm problem Newton's death cannot be explained only by handwriting or ridicule. By the late 1990s, the handheld market had changed around it. Palm showed that a PDA did not need to understand natural handwriting, reinvent mobile computing, or become an intelligent assistant. That fate shows how product categories can die not because every product inside them lacks merit, but because the company no longer wants to carry the architecture. Apple in 1998 was not looking for interesting side roads. It was looking for survival and focus. Newton OS had become a distraction. The eMate became collateral. Steve Jobs and the anti-stylus myth No Newton chapter can avoid Steve Jobs, but it should not become a simple story of Jobs killing a bad product. Jobs disliked the Newton for several reasons, some practical and some symbolic. It belonged to the Sculley era. It used a stylus. It represented a branch of Apple that had grown while Jobs was gone. It was a separate operating system at a time when Jobs wanted focus. It was a product line with loyalists but not enough market power. It consumed engineering attention in a company that needed to simplify. Later accounts, including Walter Isaacson's biography as cited by Wired, describe Jobs mocking the stylus and seeing the Newton as mismanaged despite containing good technology. Jobs would later say shutting it down freed engineers who could work on future mobile devices. This is one of the places where the mythology gets tempting. It is easy to draw a straight line: Jobs hated styluses, killed Newton, then later made the finger-driven iPhone. But the line is too clean. Apple would later sell an Apple Pencil. The iPad would eventually embrace stylus input for art, notes, and precision. The real issue was not simply pen versus finger. It was whether the stylus was required for basic use. Newton required the pen as the central mode of interaction. The iPhone would make the finger central and the stylus unnecessary.

Page 35

That difference is enormous. A stylus can be powerful when optional. It can be burdensome when mandatory. Newton's pen promised natural writing but made the device a two-handed object. The iPhone's finger interface promised direct manipulation: touch the thing you want. Later Apple could add a pencil for specialized tasks without making every user carry one. The lesson was not "styluses are bad." The lesson was "the main interface must match the mainstream moment." Newton's main interface arrived before the technology could support the promise. A failure with descendants The Newton died, but its descendants are everywhere. Not direct descendants, necessarily. The iPhone did not simply resurrect Newton. Palm, BlackBerry, iPod, Nokia, Japanese mobile internet, capacitive touchscreens, app stores, mobile browsers, and carrier negotiations all mattered. But Newton left behind ideas that became easier to see after the smartphone matured. Persistent personal information. A pocketable computing architecture. A device that could understand dates, names, and intent. A mobile operating system designed around low power. A user interface built for quick interaction outside the office. Pen and touch as ways to move beyond the keyboard. The ARM thread alone is enough to keep Newton historically important. The world's smartphones would later run overwhelmingly on ARM-derived architectures, and Newton helped push Apple toward the low-power processor decisions that made such a handheld plausible in the first place. But there is also a subtler legacy: Newton made the idea of the personal digital assistant emotionally vivid. It gave people a word, an image, and a failed example. It showed what was exciting about the category and what could go wrong. It taught the industry that intelligence was dangerous to promise before it was invisible in use. A digital assistant that announces its intelligence invites users to test it. A digital assistant that quietly parses a date, suggests a reminder, autocorrects a word, fills in a contact, or maps an address can succeed without drama.

Page 36

The smartphone era would be full of quiet Newtons. Every calendar parser that turns a message into an event, every handwriting feature that works well enough in a constrained context, every note app that syncs across devices, every assistant that tries to infer intent from language, every mobile chip designed around performance per watt carries a little of the Newton's ghost. Not because the Newton succeeded. Because it tried too much in public. The wrong lesson The shallow lesson of Newton is that Apple should not have built it. The better lesson is that the future often arrives first in forms that cannot survive. The Newton was too expensive, too early, too ambitious, too dependent on a feature that did not work well enough at launch, and too strange for a mainstream audience that had not yet learned to carry digital memory everywhere. It asked users to pay computer prices for a device whose most visible comparison was a notebook. It asked reviewers to judge a new category before the category had norms. It asked developers to trust a platform before the market had proved it. It asked Apple to support a separate mobile operating system during a period when Apple itself was struggling to stay coherent. Those are real failures. But the Newton was not foolish. It saw that the pocket would become a place for computation. It saw that mobile devices needed their own architecture. It saw that personal data mattered. It saw that handwriting, notes, calendars, contacts, and intent recognition belonged together. It saw that a device could be more than a calculator, more than a phone, more than an address book. It saw the outline of the object in your hand. The problem was that it saw too much of it at once. The next chapter in the smartphone's prehistory would push the dream even further. If Newton imagined a personal digital assistant, General Magic imagined a networked personal communicator: a small, intimate life-support system that could connect people, messages, agents, and services in a pocket-sized world. It, too, would be remembered as a company that saw the future too early. But where Newton's wound was handwriting, General Magic's wound would be infrastructure.

Chapter 3 General Magic and the Future That Arrived Too Early The night before the demonstration, the future was still misbehaving. On the stage of the Yerba Buena Room at the San Francisco Marriott, technicians moved around a setup that had the uneasy look of a magic trick not yet ready for an audience. There were Macs, prototype handheld devices, cameras positioned overhead to project tiny screens to the room, and banners carrying two names that almost nobody outside the company yet understood: Telescript and Magic Cap. The next morning, at Macworld Expo in January 1994, General Magic would give its first public demonstration. For the people waiting in that room, it was not just another product demo. It was a second chance to define what personal computing could become. Steven Levy's 1994 Wired profile described Andy Hertzfeld and Bill Atkinson in that room, both veterans of the original Macintosh team, watching another long project reach the dangerous point where private conviction had to become public evidence. A decade earlier, the Macintosh had been the beautiful wager: that ordinary people would prefer graphics, windows, icons, and a mouse to command lines and green text. Now the same kind of people, some of them literally the same people, were trying to define a more intimate machine. Not a computer for a desk. Not a portable computer for a briefcase. Not a phone in the old sense. Something smaller and stranger: a personal communicator, a digital pocket companion, an object that would organize your life, carry messages, reach services, and move through networks on your behalf. General Magic's own mission statement, written in 1990, imagined "small, intimate life support systems" that people would carry everywhere. These systems would help people organize their lives, communicate, and access information; they would come in different models and fit different budgets; they would change how people lived and communicated. It is hard to read those words now without seeing the smartphone. That is the danger of this chapter. General Magic is one of those companies that makes hindsight feel supernatural. So many later things seem to flicker inside its story: touchscreen handhelds, animated messages, network services, e-commerce, software agents, app stores, cloud computing, emojis, mobile messaging, voice assistants, even the talent diaspora that would help shape Apple, Android, Google, Nest, LinkedIn, and other parts of the modern technology world. A 2026 Wired excerpt from David Epstein's Inside the Box describes Marc Porat's 1989 "Pocket Crystal" sketch as a thin touch-screen object that combined phone, fax, text messaging, video, games, ticket-buying, downloadable apps, beauty, and pocketability -- a startlingly smartphone-like vision formed before the web existed as a mass medium.

Page 39

But this is not a story about a company that "invented the iPhone" and somehow misplaced it. That framing is too simple and too generous. General Magic saw a future that was real, but it did not build the product that made that future inevitable. It mistook a prophecy for a market. It gathered the right dreams before the surrounding world could carry them. It had talent, partners, money, charisma, and a vision so good that people still talk about it with awe. What it did not have was a small enough product, a cheap enough product, a clear enough customer, a mature enough network, or enough discipline to leave the wrong beautiful things out. The future was present. The future was not yet usable. Pocket Crystal Marc Porat did not arrive at the idea of General Magic as a gadgeteer tinkering with a toy. He came to it as someone already trained to think historically about information. Porat had studied the "information economy" before that phrase became ordinary. A Wired excerpt from Epstein's book notes that Porat used the term in his 1976 Stanford dissertation, where he analyzed the American labor force and argued that the economy was shifting from transforming matter and energy toward transforming information. In 1980, he hosted a PBS documentary called The Information Society, talking about technology's power and its risks: privacy, overload, misinformation, inequality. By the late 1980s, he was at Apple's Advanced Technology Group, a place where imagining the next thing after the personal computer was not merely permitted but expected. The story of Porat duct-taping a Sharp Wizard to a Motorola analog cell phone is almost too perfect, but it captures the moment. The electronic organizer and the mobile phone were still separate objects. One remembered; the other connected. One belonged to personal order; the other belonged to communication. Put them together, and something new appeared. Not yet a working product. Not yet a market. But a theory. The theory was that the next personal computer would not sit on a desk. It would be beautiful. It would be carried. It would be personal even when turned off. It would not be judged only by what it could calculate, but by how it felt in the hand and how deeply it entered daily life. Porat's Pocket Crystal notes, according to Wired's 2026 excerpt, described a device with the emotional value of jewelry, a touchstone, and a crystal. This was not a normal technology product brief. It was almost devotional. That mattered. General Magic was not born from a narrow product requirement. It was born from a comprehensive vision: hardware, software, telecommunications, consumer

Where Newton imagined an assistant that could understand what you wrote, General Magic imagined a communicator that could move your intentions through a digital world. It was a bigger dream than Newton's. In some ways, it was a better dream. It was also harder. Apple, Porat and Sculley concluded, could not build the whole thing alone. The vision required phone companies, electronics manufacturers, network operators, component suppliers, software developers, and service providers. It required an ecosystem before the word had become the lazy default for every technology pitch. General Magic would spin out from Apple in 1990. Apple would hold a stake. Apple wanted a stake in the next wave without fully owning the burden. Investors wanted to own a piece of the next platform. Reporters wanted the sequel to the Macintosh. The company was supposed to build a pocket communicator. It first had to manage a miniature United Nations. The Magicians The recruitment story at General Magic had the atmosphere of a legend before the product even existed. Bill Atkinson and Andy Hertzfeld were not merely experienced engineers. They were building not only software but enchantment: little rooms, animated envelopes, playful icons, messages that felt like artifacts, tools that could be touched rather than commanded. The office had the lore of a startup that was rich in confidence before it was rich in customers. Magic Cap Magic Cap was the part of General Magic's work that ordinary users would actually touch. If Newton's interface felt like a notebook that wanted to recognize your handwriting, Magic Cap felt more like a little illustrated world. It used a room and office metaphor. A user might begin at a desk, go to an office, visit a file cabinet, move down a hallway, or travel to a kind of downtown street with services and stores. The interface drew heavily on the Macintosh tradition of visual metaphor, but it was less austere than the Mac. It was cute, warm, sometimes whimsical to the point of risk. That whimsy was not incidental. General Magic was trying to make communications feel social and ordinary. Instead of presenting the user with file systems and protocol settings, it presented postcards, stamps, offices, rooms, and places. The company wanted the network to feel inhabited. Levy's 1994 Wired profile described early General Magic work around a postcard metaphor -- a message that could "drop" into a pocket -- and noted Susan Kare's graphic imprint on the interface. Magic Cap, formally short for Magic Communicating Applications Platform, was the visually oriented operating system that would run on devices made by partners such as Sony and Motorola. It was designed to work with Telescript, the deeper communications technology underneath. The interface made a bet: that people would prefer a tiny world to a tiny computer. This was not crazy. Later smartphones would be full of skeuomorphism: calendars that looked like paper, notes that looked like legal pads, folders that looked like folders, mailboxes, camera shutters, trash cans, desktops. In the early smartphone era, skeuomorphic design helped people understand unfamiliar functions by borrowing the emotional language of familiar objects. General Magic was early to that instinct. In some ways, it was more theatrical about it than Apple would later be. There was a room for work, a place for games, places for messages, and a street for shopping and services. The machine tried to make the network legible by turning it into geography. But geography takes time to traverse. One problem with metaphor-rich interfaces is that the metaphor can become overhead. A desk is friendly until the user needs to get something done quickly. A hallway is charming until every action requires walking somewhere. A room is comforting until the user forgets which room contains the tool. A virtual world can make computing feel humane, but it can also make computing feel slow.

A later smartphone home screen would be much more ruthless: icons in a grid, then search, then gestures, then notifications, then app-switching. Less charm, more immediacy. Magic Cap's world was lovely because General Magic was trying to define the future as a place. But pocket devices are often used in moments, not sessions. They reward directness. The Magic Cap interface had another strategic complication. It did not rely on handwriting recognition. This was sensible in the wake of Newton's humiliation. Levy's 1995 Wired review of Sony's Magic Link praised General Magic for avoiding the "dead-end" handwriting-recognition fight and using a virtual keyboard and touch-screen icons instead. But in the mid-1990s PDA market, handwriting recognition had also become the publicly visible measure of futuristic capability. Newton had been mocked for doing it poorly; Magic Cap risked being seen as avoiding it altogether. General Magic had made the more practical decision. The market had not yet learned to reward it. Telescript Magic Cap was the face. Telescript was the deeper dream. If Magic Cap imagined a friendly pocket world, Telescript imagined a network where software agents could travel, perform tasks, and return with results. The idea came from Jim White, a telecom programmer whose thinking shifted the company's center of gravity. Hertzfeld later told Levy that he initially thought the interface was the most important thing General Magic was doing, but after seeing White's Telescript vision, he realized the communications infrastructure was the enabling piece. In ordinary terms, Telescript was a programming language and communications system for mobile agents. A device with limited power could send a little program into the network; that program could act on the user's behalf, go to a service, complete a task, and report back. In a world of slow connections and weak handheld devices, this made conceptual sense. Rather than keep a tiny device connected while it struggled to do everything locally, send the work into the network. The idea was elegant. It was also early in exactly the wrong way. Telescript assumed a networked world rich enough to host those agents. It required participating services, trusted execution environments, authorization, security, standards, and adoption by multiple companies. It was not enough for Sony to ship a handheld. The "places" Telescript agents visited had to exist. The businesses had to be present. The network had to be reliable. The consumer had to understand why any of this mattered. The carrier

had to support the services. Developers had to build for it. Companies had to agree to a new communications layer in a decade that was about to be reorganized by the open web. A closed, agent-based communications platform might have seemed like the future in 1993. By 1996, the web made many closed futures feel old. This is one of General Magic's tragedies. Telescript did not fail because it lacked imagination. It failed partly because it imagined a structured, commercial, agent-mediated network just as the messy, open, browser-mediated internet began racing ahead. The company was trying to build a carefully designed "town center" for electronic life. Meanwhile, the web was becoming the city. AT&T's PersonaLink service was supposed to host the early Magic Cap devices. Wired's 1994 profile described PersonaLink as a kind of electronic community or town center for customers, merchants, and communication. Email was not yet universal. Consumer online services were fragmented. America Online, CompuServe, Prodigy, corporate email, dial-up modems, phone lines, pagers, and early web browsers coexisted in a confusing communications landscape. General Magic wanted to make communication feel like sending a postcard through a friendly place. But postcards need a postal system. General Magic had to build the stationery, the mailbox, the post office, the street, the stores, the delivery service, and the habit of using them. The alliance problem The company knew it needed partners. It may not have realized that partners could become another form of complexity. Apple, Sony, Motorola, AT&T, Philips, Matsushita/Panasonic, NTT, France Telecom, Toshiba, and other global firms were connected to the General Magic alliance at various points. Wired's 2026 excerpt describes alliance meetings so large and sensitive that antitrust counsel had to remind participants what they could not discuss. This is both funny and revealing. A startup normally struggles to attract the attention of giants. General Magic attracted so many giants that it had to manage giant-company politics before its first consumer product

Telecom companies wanted to shape the coming digital network. Consumer electronics companies wanted to avoid being trapped by computer companies. Everyone wanted the future, but not necessarily the same future. The alliance gave General Magic credibility. It also gave the company a false sense of inevitability. If the biggest companies in communications and electronics believed in the vision, how could the vision be wrong? If the Mac heroes were building the interface, if Porat had the strategy, if AT&T had the network, if Sony and Motorola had the devices, if investors wanted in, if the press saw the next great platform -- surely the market would arrive. But markets do not arrive because partners attend meetings. They arrive because people buy products and keep using them. General Magic's partners could not solve the user problem by existing. They could manufacture devices, host services, invest money, and lend logos. They could not make a bulky, expensive, weakly connected handheld feel necessary to ordinary people in 1994. The company's alliance strategy also created a coordination burden. A modern smartphone succeeds partly because one integrated chain can make decisions quickly, or because a dominant platform owner sets rules and everyone else adapts. Apple would later do this through vertical control. Google would do it through Android licensing and services. General Magic tried to coordinate a future among equals. A future among equals sounds democratic. It is hard to ship. The product appears When Sony's Magic Link finally appeared, it was close enough to the dream to be fascinating and far enough from the dream to be doomed. Wired's January 1995 review treated the Magic Link with a mixture of affection, excitement, and exasperation. The device could handle appointments, names, expense tracking, email downloads, brief faxes, and paging alerts. It could beam a business card to another Magic Link through infrared. It could even control a television. It used Magic Cap's friendly interface, avoided handwriting recognition, and worked with AT&T PersonaLink. Those features sound impressive because they were impressive.

General Magic had made a device that could show the future, but it could not yet deliver the future in the body that the future required. It needed a phone line or add-ons. Close is sometimes worse than far away. If a product is obviously impossible, people dismiss it and move on. If it is close, they can see exactly what is missing. A smartphone is not merely a portable computer. It is a networked pocket computer whose connectivity is assumed. Who was supposed to buy this thing? The answer shifted. It was for ordinary people. It was for "Joe Sixpack," as later accounts summarize the company's vague mass-market target. It was for professionals. It was for the information elite. It was for people who wanted email, fax, appointments, messages, services, games, electronic commerce, and networked life in a handheld object. The Palm Pilot would serve people who wanted calendars and contacts fast. BlackBerry would serve professionals who needed instant email. They did not have one thing ordinary people urgently needed at that price. This is not a moral failing. It is a common failure mode of visionary products. When a team sees too much, it becomes difficult to choose the small, boring, commercially useful first step. The emotionally satisfying thing is to build the future. That sentence hurts because it runs against the normal romance of invention. We like to think freedom produces greatness. Sometimes it does. But a product is not a dream. It is a set of exclusions. A shipped product is made from all the things the team decided not to do. General Magic had trouble not doing. The internet arrives General Magic's timing problem had many parts: batteries, screens, radios, component costs, product size, user habits, pricing, carrier infrastructure, and partner coordination. But the biggest timing problem may have been the internet. The company's vision of networked services made sense in a world of proprietary online systems and telecom-controlled services. PersonaLink could be imagined as a town center because there was not yet an obvious universal town. Consumers were using dial-up services. Businesses had corporate networks. Email systems did not all interoperate cleanly. The web had not yet eaten the interface to information. Then the browser rose. The web did not need General Magic's alliance. It did not need a special handheld. It did not need an agent language to become useful. It spread messily across existing computers and networks. It offered a simpler bargain: pages, links, browsers, servers. It was ugly, inconsistent, and less elegant than a designed electronic town. But it was open enough, cheap enough, and easy enough for millions of people and businesses to begin using. Telescript was more conceptually refined than early web pages. That did not matter. The web had a lower coordination cost. Anyone could put up a page. Anyone could point a browser at it. Standards emerged, fought, broke, and evolved in public. The web made the network useful before it made the network beautiful. General Magic wanted to make the network beautiful before enough of the network existed. By the time the first products reached consumers, the center of gravity was already shifting. A closed service like PersonaLink looked less like the future and more like one of

The smartphone would later reunite some of General Magic's instincts with the internet's reality: apps, stores, messaging, agents, assistants, cloud services, and commerce would all live on top of an open-ish web and mobile broadband world. But in the mid-1990s, the pieces had not yet converged. General Magic's town center was being built just as the world discovered the city outside. Why Newton's failure hurt General Magic Newton and General Magic are usually paired as two early failures in the pre-smartphone lineage, but their relationship was more complicated than that. General Magic began as an Apple project called Pocket Crystal before spinning out. Newton emerged inside Apple and shifted toward the same broad PDA/personal communicator territory. General Magic people watched Apple’s Newton move into their conceptual space. Apple, a shareholder and early parent, became a competitor. Levy's 1994 Wired profile captured the sense of betrayal and tension among old Apple colleagues as Newton's positioning began to overlap with General Magic's vision. Newton's failure then created a market problem for everyone. When Newton struggled, it did not merely damage Apple. It damaged the idea of the PDA. It made the category feel premature. General Magic had made a wise decision to avoid depending on handwriting recognition, but the public conversation around handheld digital assistants had already been shaped by Newton's flaws. Reviewers, consumers, and investors were primed to ask whether these devices worked yet. In one sense, Newton failed because it promised too much intelligence. General Magic failed because it promised too much world. Both suffered from the same historical condition: the future was visible but not yet cheap, small, fast, connected, reliable, and socially necessary enough. Newton's wound was in the interface. General Magic's wound was in the infrastructure. But the market did not separate the wounds cleanly. To many people, they were both examples of the same thing: expensive little machines that did not yet justify their drama. The product that needed another product The Magic Link wanted a built-in phone.

Its alliance was impressive but cumbersome. Its culture celebrated invention more than constraint. Its first consumer product did not solve a narrow urgent problem. It was overtaken by the web's simpler, more open momentum. It let the future become too big for the first product to carry. That does not make the company less important. It makes it more useful. A pure timing failure teaches only patience. General Magic teaches something sharper: the future usually arrives through constraint. Listen to Atkinson's remark, recorded by Levy, imagining a personal communicator that would become as essential as glasses, a watch, or a wallet. That is the smartphone. Not the hardware, not the exact interface, not the business model, not the winning platform. But the social position. The psychic location. The object that moves from gadget to necessity. The next great handheld success would not try to build a complete networked life-support system. It would be humbler. Chapter 4 Palm and the Beauty of Doing Less Jeff Hawkins carried the future in his pocket before the future did anything. It was not a computer. It was not a prototype in the usual sense. It had no screen, no processor, no batteries, no operating system, no handwriting recognition, no memory, no cradle, no calendar, no stylus, no serial connector, no software waiting to crash. It was a block of wood. Hawkins carried it around anyway. At PalmSource in 1999, speaking to Palm developers after the little device he had imagined had already become one of the defining consumer electronics products of the decade, Hawkins described the exercise with the matter-of-factness of someone explaining a perfectly reasonable engineering method. People had thought he was crazy, he said, when they saw him taking notes, checking appointments, and pretending to synchronize a small wooden block with his PC. If he wanted to check the calendar, he would take the block out and press the wooden button. Later, while designing the Handspring handheld, he walked around pretending to answer phone calls with another block, just to see whether the behavior made sense. That sounds like theater. It was actually discipline. Hawkins was not testing the electronics. There were no electronics to test. He was testing the life of the object. Where would the pen go? How many steps would it take to open the date book? Could the thing be removed from a pocket while standing? Would someone actually carry it? Would the ritual be annoying? Would it survive the little frictions that kill portable devices long before their specifications have a chance to impress anyone? This was the opposite of General Magic. General Magic had imagined a pocket world: networks, agents, animated messages, electronic town centers, partner alliances, services, devices, a whole digital life-support system waiting to exist. Newton had imagined a digital assistant that could understand ordinary handwriting and infer a user’s intentions from scribbles. Palm, by contrast, began with a block of wood and the number of steps required to check today’s appointments. That difference is easy to understate. The Palm Pilot was not a triumph because it had the grandest vision. It was a triumph because it had the smallest acceptable one.

Page 57

It did not try to be the future of everything. It tried to be the thing you reached for instead of a paper calendar, an address book, a to-do list, a memo pad, or the back of your hand. It did not try to build the mobile internet. It did not try to solve speech recognition. It did not try to send software agents into a proprietary network. It did not try to read your natural handwriting. It did not even try, at first, to be a phone. It tried to fit. It tried to wake. It tried to remember. It tried to synchronize. And that was enough. The lesson after overreach By the mid-1990s, the handheld computing category had already learned how to embarrass itself. Newton had been ambitious, expensive, and publicly wounded by handwriting recognition. General Magic had been visionary, alliance-heavy, and stranded before the networks and services its products needed. IBM Simon had shown how many smartphone- like functions could be squeezed into a device that still did not have the surrounding world to make them feel natural. Psion had built serious pocket computers, especially in Europe, with keyboards, calendars, databases, programming, and a loyal user base. Windows CE would soon bring Microsoft’s familiar interface and partner strategy into handheld machines that looked more like tiny laptops than paper replacements. Palm entered this crowded, uncertain, self-conscious category with a strange advantage: it had less to prove. It did not need to show that the future could be intelligent. It needed to show that a pocket organizer could be useful. The company itself did not begin as a hardware maker. Palm Computing was founded in 1992 by Jeff Hawkins, and Donna Dubinsky and Ed Colligan became central to the company’s early leadership. Palm’s first business was not the Pilot; it made software for earlier PDA efforts, including personal information management software and handwriting-related technology for devices such as the Tandy/Casio/GRiD Zoomer. That early market had not turned into a mass success, but it taught Palm what did not work.

Page 58

The Zoomer mattered because it gave Palm a seat inside the category before Palm had the power to remake it. It also showed how a PDA could be loaded with features and still not become a daily object. The problem was not just whether engineers could assemble a small computer. They could. The problem was whether the result justified its own presence. Palm’s eventual answer was brutal in its simplicity. Make it small. Make it cheap enough. Make it fast. Make it easy to sync. Make the core applications obvious. Do not make the user wait. Do not make the user admire the architecture before getting value. Do not make the user carry a future that is heavier than the present. Hawkins later summarized his philosophy with a line that reads like an insult to specification sheets: “To heck with specs.” At PalmSource, he said he cared less about screen size and chip speed than about where the pen lived, where the buttons went, and how many steps it took to open the date book. This was not anti-technology. It was anti-distraction. A handheld device lives or dies in the first few seconds of use. A laptop can ask for patience. A desktop computer can take over a desk. A network service can explain itself with manuals, demos, and support lines. A pocket device does not have that luxury. It has to answer a small question before the user decides reaching for it was a mistake. What time is the meeting? What is the number? What did I need to buy? What did I promise to do? What was that idea? Palm understood that these were not “small” use cases. They were the daily administrative burden of being a person. The Palm Pilot did not make the pocket computer grander.

Page 59

The Psion was not Palm’s opposite. It was Palm’s cousin from another design culture. Open a Psion and it looked like a tiny computer. It had a keyboard, a wide screen, menus, applications, files, and a more explicit sense of computing. It could write, calculate, store records, manage an agenda, and in the hands of enthusiasts, run programs made by its users. It respected the user who wanted a computer small enough to carry but still recognizably a computer. Palm respected a different user, or perhaps the same user in a different moment. A Palm Pilot did not unfold into a small workbench. It did not ask you to type. It did not look like a miniature laptop. It was a slab. The face of the device had a square monochrome screen, a writing area for Graffiti, a power button, four application buttons, and scroll controls. Date Book. Address Book. To Do List. Memo Pad. Psion users could live inside the machine for longer sessions. Palm users dipped in and out. The Palm’s virtue was not depth in a single sitting. It was repetition without irritation. Pull it out. Tap the button. Check the date. Add a name. Write a note. Put it back. Later, drop it into the cradle. Press the sync button. A device designed for moments can beat a device designed for sessions, even if the session device is more capable.

Paper, improved reluctantly The most revealing launch-window review of the Pilot began not by praising the device, but by praising paper. The enemy was paper. Paper had almost everything a handheld organizer wanted: instant access, zero boot time, infinite flexibility, low price, no batteries, no learning curve, high social acceptance, and broad compatibility with pockets, desks, bags, and human hands. Paper could be ugly and still useful. It could be folded, torn, circled, scribbled on, highlighted, lost, found, and replaced. It was not intelligent, but it was forgiving. A digital organizer had to offer enough improvement to overcome paper’s grace. Palm did not try to beat paper at everything. It beat paper at the things paper did poorly. Search. Duplication. Synchronization. Alarms. Recurring appointments. Bulk. Legibility, at least after data entry. A paper address book grows messy. Names move. Numbers change. This was the point of HotSync. The button that mattered The cradle looked mundane. The Palm Pilot would sit in it like a little appliance returning to base. It was not. It was one of the most important acts of simplification in the pre-smartphone era. The Palm did not require the user to understand file transfer. It did not turn synchronization into a technical ceremony. It made sync a physical gesture. Drop the device in the cradle. Press the button. The same July 1996 Wired review captured the relief of this. Keeping data synchronized between a palmtop and a desktop, the reviewer wrote, had always felt like “two-timing,” with appointments and notes scattered between devices. It was a companion to the PC. But unlike many companions, it knew its place. The PC could remain the larger storehouse, the place for longer editing, backups, and more comfortable input. The Palm would be the pocket copy, the moment machine, the glanceable memory. Synchronization allowed the Palm to avoid the burden of becoming a complete computer while still feeling connected to one. HotSync also created trust. Trust is a practical emotion. Graffiti, or the humility of making the user learn Newton had tried to recognize ordinary handwriting. Palm chose a more modest bargain: learn our alphabet, and the machine will recognize you. This was Graffiti. Graffiti was not natural handwriting in the romantic sense. It was a simplified, mostly single-stroke shorthand for letters and numbers, entered with a stylus in a dedicated writing area. The user wrote characters one at a time, often on top of each other, rather than across a line. The system reduced ambiguity by simplifying the shapes. It did not promise that the machine would adapt completely to the user. It asked the user to adapt slightly to the machine. This was a radical act of restraint. After Newton, asking people to learn special handwriting could have sounded like a step backward. But it solved the problem Newton had made famous. Graffiti did not need to parse every quirk of ordinary handwriting. It narrowed the input space. The machine could recognize a more predictable set of gestures, and the user could get faster with practice. Hawkins later described how he tested the idea by scribbling notes all day on paper, but writing the letters on top of one another as they would be written on the Palm. The result on paper was an indecipherable blob, but it gave him a feel for how the interaction would work. This matters because Graffiti was not merely a technical system. It was a design philosophy. Four buttons and a theory of life The four application buttons on the face of the Pilot were more important than they looked. Date Book. Address Book. To Do List. Memo Pad. Each button was a theory of what belonged in the pocket. Date Book meant time. Not abstract time, but social time: meetings, lunches, flights, calls, deadlines, birthdays, appointments, reminders. A calendar is not just a list of hours. It is a map of obligation.

It told you who could be reached and how. To Do List meant intention. The small promises a person makes to themselves and others. Buy batteries. Call Susan. Send invoice. Book hotel. Check contract. Follow up. Memo Pad meant capture. The stray thought, meeting note, quote, idea, password, book recommendation, parking location, or number scribbled before it disappeared. Palm did not invent these categories. It recognized that they were the minimum viable contents of a mobile life. The genius was not that these applications were advanced. The genius was that they were right there, one press away. Hawkins later mocked competitors for copying Palm’s buttons without understanding them, noting that the Date Book button still needed to take the user to today. That detail is everything. A calendar button that does not take you to today is a button designed by someone thinking about an application. A calendar button that takes you to today is designed by someone thinking about a person standing in a hallway. Palm’s most important design choices were like that: small, almost invisible, and humanly exact. It is tempting, in a history of smartphones, to focus on the spectacular breakthroughs: multi-touch, app stores, cameras, broadband networks, mobile chips, cloud synchronization. But the smartphone’s deeper ancestry lies in small decisions like the Date Book button. Mobile computing is full of apparently minor interface choices that decide whether a device becomes habit. A habit does not need to be admired every time. It needs to disappear. Windows in the pocket Palm’s restraint became more visible as competitors took the opposite path. A small machine with Pocket Word, Pocket Excel, a keyboard, file structures, and a Windows- like interface could look more serious than a Palm Pilot. Seriousness was Microsoft’s advantage. It was also a burden. A Windows CE handheld promised a little computer. That meant users could judge it as a computer. Was the keyboard good enough? Was the screen large enough? No giant developer keynote staged for millions. No glossy app icons bouncing into an ecosystem. But the ingredients were there. The device had an operating system. It had a consistent form factor. It had a growing user base. It had synchronization with desktop computers. It had a developer community. It had enough simplicity that third-party software could make the device feel larger without making the core experience collapse. A cheaper rival can look attractive until the buyer asks what else they can do with it. Palm’s ecosystem was not yet the app economy. The hardware remained modest. The surrounding world made it grow.

The iPhone’s App Store would not succeed merely because people wanted infinite software. It would succeed because the phone’s basic experience was coherent enough that software could extend it. Android’s scale would depend not only on openness but on a recognizable base. A platform that is confusing before extensions becomes worse with them. Palm’s smallness made expansion tolerable. The emotional satisfaction of fast The Palm Pilot was not just useful. It was satisfying. This is a different claim. Usefulness is about outcomes. Satisfaction is about feel. A device can be useful and still unpleasant. Many early mobile devices were like that: worth the hassle only if the need was strong enough. Palm lowered the hassle until the use itself became rewarding. Part of that satisfaction was speed. The Palm woke quickly. The core applications opened quickly. The buttons did what they looked like they should do. The screen was simple enough to draw fast. The operating system did not burden the moment with ceremony. Part of it was size. The device fit a pocket in a way Newton had only aspired to. It could be carried without announcing itself as a professional burden. It was not weightless, but it was plausible. Part of it was battery behavior. Two AAA batteries belonged to the ordinary world. They could be bought anywhere. The device was not psychologically tied to a charging ritual in the same way later phones would be. A user could understand the battery. That mattered. Part of it was the stylus. Unlike Newton, where the stylus was linked to the public embarrassment of failed handwriting recognition, the Palm stylus became a tool for a constrained, learnable system. It tapped. It selected. It wrote Graffiti. It lived in a slot. It made the device feel precise rather than overpromising. Part of it was modesty. The Pilot did not constantly reveal what it could not do. It mostly stayed inside what it could. That is why it became lovable.

Page 69

Palm users often became evangelists because the device solved many small irritations instead of one spectacular problem. It did not transform a life in a single demo. It transformed life by being pulled out fifty times a week without causing resentment. The best mobile devices accumulate affection through reliability. Each successful small interaction says: yes, I belong in your pocket. Donna Dubinsky and the business of making simplicity real The Palm story is often told through Hawkins because the wooden block is irresistible and because his design philosophy is so vivid. But the device did not become an industry by design philosophy alone. Donna Dubinsky mattered because Palm needed a company, not only a product idea. She had worked at Apple, had run Claris, and brought operational seriousness to a young company trying to survive after early PDA disappointments. Later accounts emphasize her role as Palm’s founding chief executive and the person charged with raising money, building the company, and developing the sales strategy. Palm sold to U.S. Robotics in 1995 for $44 million, shortly before the Pilot went on sale. That sequence matters. A beautiful small product still has to be manufactured, priced, sold, supported, distributed, and defended. Palm’s simplicity was not a design artifact alone. It required organizational choices. A company had to resist feature pressure. It had to ship at a price that made sense. It had to partner with a larger company when necessary. It had to convince retailers and users that the device was not another doomed PDA experiment. It had to build trust after Newton had made the whole category look risky. Dubinsky’s presence also complicates the myth of the lone product visionary. Hawkins had the block of wood and the interface philosophy. Ed Colligan helped shape the product and marketing vision. Dubinsky brought leadership, capital strategy, and business discipline. The Pilot succeeded because the idea became a company capable of shipping and selling it. Technology history often remembers the person holding the prototype. But prototypes do not reach pockets by themselves. The name problem The product name itself became part of the story.

Page 70

The first devices were simply Pilots, but Pilot Pen Corporation objected to the use of “Pilot.” After a trademark dispute, Palm stopped using the Pilot name for later devices, and the products became Palm organizers or Palm-branded handhelds. The public, however, kept saying PalmPilot for years, even as the official naming shifted. This is a small legal footnote with a cultural lesson inside it. Users do not always follow branding. They keep the names that make sense. “PalmPilot” had exactly the right rhythm: palm for the hand, pilot for guidance, a little navigator for daily life. Even after the company moved away from the word, it stuck because it described what the device felt like. It was not quite a computer. It was not quite an organizer. It was a pilot for the palm. That stickiness helped the category. People who did not know model numbers knew the type. A PalmPilot was a thing. To see one was to understand, roughly, what kind of person used it: someone organized, perhaps a little gadget-proud, perhaps busy, perhaps the kind of person who had stopped trusting paper. The brand acquired social meaning. And social meaning is another step toward the smartphone. Competition gets serious By 1998, the handheld market had become important enough for everyone to react. Microsoft pushed Windows CE and then Palm-size PCs, though even the name “Palm-size PC” created trademark tension with 3Com. Windows CE devices promised more features, more familiarity, more corporate seriousness, and in some cases larger screens, keyboards, or communications options. Low-cost competitors tried to undercut Palm on price. Royal’s Da Vinci, for example, was positioned as a cheaper handheld organizer, with a basic model expected around $100 and a more advanced model around $150. But the challenge was not only price. As Wired reported, Palm had a software and shareware ecosystem around it, and even competitors with better or cheaper hardware had to catch up to the maturity of the Palm world. Internationally, Microsoft tried to use Windows CE’s localization and partner model to move into markets where Palm was less established. In 1998, Wired reported Microsoft’s plans for a Chinese-language version of Windows CE, with manufacturers such as Philips and Legend expected to use the platform, and devices projected around $350 to $420. Another Wired report described Microsoft’s attempt to enter China with Windows CE handhelds before PalmPilot became familiar there.

Page 71

These efforts show how Palm’s success changed the market. Before Palm, the PDA category had to justify its existence. After Palm, competitors had to explain why their organizer was better than a Palm. That is a major shift. Palm had become the reference object. Its competitors often offered more: more features, more desktop-like applications, more hardware expansion, more communications options, sometimes lower prices. But Palm had the harder-to-copy advantage of rightness. Its design had matured around repeated use. Its limitations had become part of its identity. Its software ecosystem had grown around its constraints. The industry kept trying to beat Palm by doing more. Palm kept winning because, for the moment, less was what users needed. The first computer that did not feel like one Palm’s greatest contribution to the smartphone was not any single technology. It was the proof that a pocket computer could become ordinary without pretending to be a desktop computer. This seems obvious now because phones are computers that do not usually feel like computers. But in the 1990s, many devices still carried the psychological burden of computing. They asked users to manage files, think about memory, choose applications, configure connections, tolerate slow startup, learn operating-system behavior, and forgive instability. Palm made the computer feel like an appliance. That appliance quality mattered enormously. A user did not need to understand Palm OS to use a Palm. The operating system mostly stayed out of the way. The applications were plain. The buttons were physical. The sync button was obvious. The screen was not beautiful, but it was readable enough. The whole device felt as though it had a job. This was one of the first mainstream successes of post-desktop computing. Not post-PC in the later marketing sense. The Palm still depended on the PC. But it was post-desktop in the experience sense. It took a kind of computing out of the desk and made it behave according to pocket rules. Those pocket rules would later become smartphone rules:

Page 72

Start quickly. Do not waste the user’s attention. Make the most common tasks one touch away. Sync personal data. Treat contacts and calendar as core infrastructure. Let third-party software expand the device. Fit the hand before impressing the spec sheet. Design for moments. Palm did not solve every smartphone problem. It avoided many of them. It did not solve mobile internet. It did not solve voice communication. It did not solve rich media. It did not solve camera integration. It did not solve wireless email in the way BlackBerry would. It did not solve carrier distribution. It did not solve the convergence of phone and PDA. But by avoiding those problems, it solved one that earlier products had made harder than it needed to be: How do you make a pocket computer people actually use? The shadow of success Success created its own pressures. Palm had become valuable enough to be pulled through corporate structures that its founders did not control. U.S. Robotics acquired Palm in 1995. 3Com acquired U.S. Robotics in 1997. By 1998, Hawkins, Dubinsky, and Colligan were frustrated enough with 3Com’s direction to leave and found Handspring. Wired reported in November 1998 that the creators of the original PalmPilot had announced funding for Handspring from Kleiner Perkins and Benchmark, and that the new company intended to target consumers with handheld computers and software while licensing PalmPilot technology for compatibility. That departure points forward. Palm’s original act of restraint had made a category real. But once the category was real, the next questions arrived. Could a handheld become a consumer product, not just a business organizer? Could it expand without losing its simplicity? Could it connect more directly? Could it become a phone? Could it move from personal information management to personal communication?

Page 73

Handspring would explore those questions. Its Visor would add expansion through the Springboard slot. Its later Treo would become one of the most important early smartphone lines, combining Palm-like PDA behavior with phone functions. Hawkins’s second wooden- block experiment — pretending to answer phone calls with a handheld to see whether it worked — was not a joke. It was the beginning of the next convergence. Palm itself would continue to rise. By early 2000, as Palm prepared for its IPO, Wired reported that the company had sold more than five million Palm computers and held more than two-thirds of the world market for handheld computing devices. The same report noted Palm’s profitability and the excitement around its public offering, even as 3Com still owned it. That kind of success attracts two things: competitors and strategic confusion. Palm had won the PDA market by doing less. But the market would not remain satisfied with less forever. The internet was spreading. Mobile phones were getting smaller and cheaper. SMS was becoming culturally important. BlackBerry was building a new kind of urgency around wireless email. Nokia was treating the phone as a global computing object. Microsoft would keep pushing handheld versions of its platform logic. And users, having accepted calendars and contacts in the pocket, would begin to ask why messages, calls, maps, and media had to live elsewhere. The device that succeeds by doing less eventually faces the danger of needing to do more. A small thing that changed expectations Palm belongs in the smartphone story not because it was secretly a smartphone. It was not. It belongs because it taught the industry that the pocket had its own grammar. The first rule was size. Not theoretical portability, not bag portability, not “it fits if you really want it to” portability. Pocket plausibility. The second rule was speed. A pocket device has to answer before impatience wins. The third rule was focus. The user must know why the device exists. The fourth rule was synchronization. A pocket computer becomes more valuable when it is not an isolated island. The fifth rule was learnable input. Perfect naturalness is less important than reliability.

Page 74

The sixth rule was emotional restraint. Do not make the user feel foolish for trusting the device. Palm’s greatness was not that it imagined the largest future. It was that it made the nearest future comfortable. This is why it should sit between General Magic and BlackBerry in the story. General Magic showed what a networked pocket communicator might someday mean. Palm showed how small the first winning step could be. BlackBerry would then show how powerful the pocket became when it was connected to a specific stream of urgent communication: email. Palm organized the pocket. BlackBerry would electrify it. And eventually the smartphone would inherit both: the Palm’s calendar, contacts, notes, and sync; the BlackBerry’s urgency, keyboard, and wireless messaging; the iPod’s pleasure; the phone’s reachability; the browser’s world; the app store’s marketplace. But before all of that, there was the wooden block. A man checking a pretend calendar. A pretend button. A pretend sync. A product designer willing to look ridiculous long enough to discover whether the pocket wanted the thing. Newton had tried to make the assistant intelligent. General Magic had tried to make the network intimate. Palm made the pocket computer boring enough to use every day. That was its brilliance. Part II Communicators Before the Smartphone Chapter 5 The Communicator Era The first thing a Nokia Communicator asked you to do was open it. That sounds ordinary now, after years of laptops, clamshell phones, folding screens, and portable machines that reveal their seriousness by splitting into two halves. But in the middle of the 1990s, the gesture still carried a little drama. Closed, the Nokia 9000 Communicator looked like a large, businesslike mobile phone, the sort of object that might live in the inside pocket of a suit jacket or the side compartment of a leather briefcase. It was long, thick, and heavy by the standards of later phones, but it was still recognizably a telephone. It had a keypad, a small exterior display, an antenna, and the plain expectation that you could lift it to your head and speak. Then it opened. Inside was not the phone world. Inside was a miniature office. A wide monochrome screen stretched across the top half. A physical QWERTY keyboard filled the lower half. Suddenly the device no longer belonged to the lineage of the handset alone; it belonged also to the lineage of the palmtop, the organizer, the portable typewriter, and the laptop. The hinge did something that later smartphones would try to do without moving parts: it changed the meaning of the object in your hand. Outside, it was a phone. Inside, it was a computer. The Nokia 9000 Communicator, released in 1996, was not subtle about its ambition. It was announced at CeBIT and introduced to the market that August. It had a GSM cellular phone, a 24 MHz Intel i386 processor, 8 MB of memory divided between applications, program memory, and user data, and a PEN/GEOS operating system on the PDA side. It could send and receive email and faxes over GSM circuit-switched data at 9.6 kilobits per second, print through infrared, browse the web in a limited way, and run business applications. Opened, it presented a 640-by-200 monochrome display and a full keyboard. It weighed 397 grams — nearly fourteen ounces — and launched in the United Kingdom at about £1,000. Those numbers matter because they make the device legible in its own time. The 9000 was not an everyday consumer phone with some extra features. It was a serious instrument for people who had a reason to carry work into motion. Its most natural owner was not a

Page 77

teenager texting under a desk or a music listener walking down the street. It was the person in an airport lounge, a hotel room, a taxi, a sales call, a train carriage, or a client meeting who needed something more than voice. It was for the user who wanted a document, a number, a calendar entry, a fax, an email, a contact list, and a phone call to occupy the same object, even if that object strained the pocket. The device belonged to a particular moment in the history of mobility. Palm had proved that personal information could live happily in the hand, but Palm was still mostly a satellite of the desktop. General Magic had imagined networked pocket life, but its devices had depended on a world that was not ready. Newton had tried to create an intelligent assistant, but its first public identity had been damaged by unreliable handwriting recognition. The Nokia Communicator took a more direct approach. It did not ask a computer to understand your natural writing. It did not ask you to enter an electronic town. It simply put a small computer and a phone into the same hinged body and dared the business traveler to tolerate the result. The Communicator era begins there, not because Nokia was alone, and not because the 9000 was the first device to combine pieces of a phone and computer, but because the 9000 made the merger feel literal. It was not a metaphor. It was a hinge. Finland, GSM, and the phone as seriousness The Communicator made more sense coming from Nokia than it might have from almost anyone else. In the late 1990s, Nokia was in the middle of one of the great corporate transformations in technology history. The company had roots in paper, rubber, cables, and consumer electronics, but by the end of the decade it had refocused around digital phones and networks. Wired's 1999 profile of Nokia described a Finnish company that had narrowed itself around mobile communications and grown into the world's leading mobile-phone manufacturer after passing Motorola. The story was inseparable from GSM, the European digital standard whose spread gave Nokia a global platform for both handsets and network infrastructure. That same profile contains one of the most evocative words in this whole history: kännykkä. In Finnish slang, Wired explained, the mobile phone had become “an extension of the hand.” The phrase sounds almost too perfect in retrospect, because the smartphone would later become exactly that for billions of people. But in Finland in the 1990s, the idea was already culturally available. Mobile phones were not only business tools; they were entering ordinary life faster than in many other markets. Nokia did not have to imagine the social legitimacy of carrying a phone. It could see it outside.

Page 78

The Nokia 9000 Communicator, though, was not yet the phone as ordinary social object. It was the phone as professional instrument. It was what happened when Nokia took the cellular phone seriously as a base for computing rather than as an accessory to it. Many American technology companies approached mobile communication from the computer side: take a PDA, add a radio, maybe add a phone. Nokia approached from the phone side: take a mobile handset, open it, and reveal a computer. That difference shaped the product. The Communicator did not feel like a PalmPilot with a phone grafted on. It felt like a phone company's idea of portable office work. It had the heft and authority of telecommunications. It carried the logic of GSM: the network was not an add-on; it was the ground. The result was ungainly and impressive in equal measure. A user could open the device, find a contact, press Call, and move from stored personal information to voice communication without copying numbers from one gadget to another. A 1998 Wired review of the Nokia 9000i described the appeal precisely: instead of carrying a cell phone, a PalmPilot, and a laptop, the user could slip the 9000i into a coat pocket. The review praised the full address book and calendar, the ability to place calls directly from the stored contact information, the web browser's SSL support, and email access through POP and IMAP. It also noted the costs of the design: a nonretractable antenna, battery life that generally lasted only half a day, and a tiny charger easy to misplace. That review captures the Communicator era in one breath. Here was the dream: one device instead of three. Here were the compromises: weight, battery, size, antenna, charger, network speed, and the reality that “portable office” still meant carrying a machine designed for people willing to forgive inconvenience in exchange for capability. The Communicator was not an elegant consumer object. It was a worksite. The hinge as worldview The hinge solved one problem and exposed another. Closed, the Communicator could be used as a phone. Open, it could be used as a computer. This was clever because it allowed the device to preserve two interaction models. A phone call did not require a keyboard and wide screen. Email and documents did. Rather than force all tasks through one cramped interface, Nokia gave the device two bodies sharing one shell. But the hinge also admitted that the phone and computer had not yet been fully reconciled. They still needed separate postures.

Page 79

This is one of the defining features of the Communicator era. Early smartphones were often less like integrated products than like negotiated settlements between categories. The PDA wanted a stylus, calendar, contacts, and notes. The phone wanted a keypad, antenna, microphone, earpiece, and battery reliability. The laptop wanted a keyboard and a wide screen. The pager wanted instant alerts. The email appliance wanted a thumb keyboard. The mobile browser wanted a screen larger than the phone could comfortably afford. The network wanted standards, billing, and carrier approval. The user wanted all of this to fit in a pocket. No single design language had yet won. So devices split themselves into modes. They flipped open. They hid keyboards. They attached modules. They covered Palm screens with phone keypads. They placed phone buttons over Graffiti areas. They used jog dials, styluses, tiny keys, scroll wheels, and soft keys. They asked users to learn which part of the device they were using at any given moment. In that sense, the Communicator era was less an era of smartphones than an era of negotiations. Every device carried an argument about what should come first. Phone first? Computer first? Keyboard first? Stylus first? Network first? Organizer first? Email first? The winning answer had not yet emerged, which is why the products of this era are so fascinating. They reveal assumptions before those assumptions hardened into convention. The Nokia Communicator assumed that a serious mobile professional wanted a miniature laptop hidden inside a phone. Palm OS phone makers assumed that users wanted their organizer to gain voice and data. Ericsson's R380 assumed that a phone could conceal PDA functions behind a flip and a touch-sensitive screen. Microsoft assumed that the mobile future would remain connected to Windows, Pocket Outlook, Pocket Internet Explorer, and a family of devices synchronized to the PC. Handspring assumed that a PDA-phone could become a smaller, more personal object if the interface were designed around one-handed use and familiar Palm habits.

Page 80

Each assumption was reasonable. None was complete. The Palm phone problem If the Communicator represented the phone company's route into computing, the Palm- phone experiments represented the PDA world trying to gain a voice. The logic was irresistible. By 1998, Palm had found the winning formula for pocket organization. Its devices were simple, fast, familiar, and increasingly surrounded by software. The missing piece was connectivity. A Palm could remember the number, but a separate phone still had to dial it. A Palm could store email, but a separate network connection still had to retrieve it. A Palm could become more useful through synchronization, but the sync cradle was still a reminder that the device came alive in bursts rather than continuously. The obvious move was to combine Palm and phone. Qualcomm's pdQ was one of the earliest American attempts to make that combination explicit. Announced in 1998, the pdQ combined a digital phone with 3Com's pen-based PalmPilot organizer. Wired described it as able to browse the net, make voice calls, send and receive email and pages, and manage appointments and contacts. The device was expected to go through market trials in late 1998 and reach sale in 1999; it used a 160-by-240 pixel touchscreen, measured roughly 15.7 by 6.7 by 3.5 centimeters, and weighed 229 grams. The pdQ was the kind of product that looked obvious once announced and awkward once held. It had Palm's organizer functions and a phone's connectivity, but combination did not automatically produce coherence. The user still had to accept the size. The phone still had to be good enough as a phone. The PDA still had to be good enough as a PDA. The network still had to make data useful enough to justify the trouble. By 2001, Kyocera's QCP 6035, descended from Qualcomm's handset business after Kyocera acquired it, offered a more mature version of the same idea. Verizon sold the Palm- powered converged PDA phone, and Wired reported that the device received heavy promotion and had sold out in some San Francisco stores soon after launch. It was priced at $500 with a Verizon plan. But the most revealing reporting on the Kyocera device was not about its sales burst. It was about reluctance. In a March 2001 Wired piece, industry observers admitted that PDA phones were not yet going to replace separate cell phones and handheld devices. A shopping- site executive who liked his Kyocera 6035 also carried Motorola and Nextel phones, a BlackBerry pager, and a Palm V. The article noted that early PDA phones were still too large and expensive for ordinary consumers, and cited survey findings suggesting many Americans preferred multiple devices if that gave them the services they wanted.

Page 81

That image — one person carrying a Kyocera smartphone, two other cell phones, a BlackBerry, and a Palm — is almost comic. It is also one of the most honest scenes in the pre- smartphone story. The whole industry was trying to converge devices, and yet the early adopter still carried a small personal electronics store on his body. The problem was not that convergence made no sense. The problem was that bad convergence could be worse than separation. A small phone was better at being a phone. A Palm V was better at being a Palm. A BlackBerry was better at mobile email. The Kyocera was impressive because it did many things. It was not yet compelling enough to let all the other objects stay home. That is the standard every early smartphone had to meet, whether its designers said it aloud or not. It had to be good enough to retire something else. Many were not. Ericsson and the word “smartphone” If Nokia made the communicator feel like a miniature office and Palm-phone makers made the PDA try to speak, Ericsson offered another route: make the phone remain phone- like, but hide the assistant inside. The Ericsson R380, introduced at CeBIT in 1999 and shipped around September 2000, combined a GSM cellular phone with PDA functions and is widely noted as the first mobile device marketed as a “Smartphone.” It had a keypad flip that opened to reveal a touch- sensitive black-and-white display, ran EPOC Release 5.1 — a forerunner of Symbian OS — and included WAP for mobile internet services. It was small and light compared with many PDA-phone hybrids, about 160 grams, and looked closer to a normal phone than a brick-like palmtop. The R380's design was important because it respected the phone's social role. Unlike the Nokia Communicator, which almost proudly became a tiny computer when opened, the R380 tried to preserve the identity of a mobile phone. Closed, it did not ask to be mistaken for a laptop. It was a handset with a secret. Open, it gave the user access to a wider screen and PDA functions. But here again, the compromise revealed itself. The R380 could be marketed as a smartphone, but users could not install their own software on it. Its operating system lineage mattered historically, but as a platform it remained limited. That limitation is crucial. Later, the smartphone would become defined not only by what came in the box but by what could be added afterward. A device that combined phone and PDA functions could be smart in the marketing sense without being a platform in the later

sense. The R380 was one of the first objects to wear the word, but the word itself was still unsettled. “Smartphone” did not yet mean full-screen app platform. It did not yet mean a pocket internet device with a software marketplace. It did not yet mean cloud backups, sensors, maps, social feeds, camera rolls, and app ecosystems. It meant, roughly: this phone knows more than a normal phone. The R380 shows how young the category still was. A phone could be called smart while remaining closed to third-party applications. A communicator could have a browser and fax but remain a niche business machine. A PDA phone could run Palm OS but still be too big and expensive to displace separate devices. The word had arrived before the expectations had stabilized. Symbian, or the alliance against Microsoft Behind many of the European communicator devices was a deeper strategic contest: who would own the operating system for mobile computing? Psion had already built capable handheld computers, and its EPOC operating system had grown out of that world. By 1998, Nokia, Ericsson, Motorola, and Psion formed Symbian Ltd., a joint venture intended to establish a widely accepted software platform for wireless information devices. Wired's coverage at the time described Symbian as a move to create an alternative to Microsoft's Windows CE, with EPOC positioned for internet access, messaging, and information transmission across new categories of devices. This alliance made strategic sense. The mobile-phone companies had seen what Microsoft had done on the desktop. They did not want the phone to become another hardware business dependent on Windows. If mobile devices were going to become computers, then the operating system was not a technical detail; it was a question of industry power. Symbian was therefore both a technology project and a defensive maneuver. It brought together companies that competed fiercely in handsets because they shared a fear of being reduced to hardware makers in a Microsoft-controlled mobile software world. Nokia, Ericsson, and Motorola did not agree about everything, but they could agree that the phone should not simply become a pocket Windows terminal. The idea was elegant in principle. In practice, it introduced the kind of complexity that would later become part of Symbian's burden. It had phone-company backing, PDA lineage, low-power design, and a clear strategic reason to exist. The 9210 was therefore closer to the later smartphone ideal than the 9000 in at least one important respect: it was a more open software platform. But physically and socially, it still belonged to the communicator class. It was not an invisible daily object. It was a device for people who wanted the computer inside the phone strongly enough to accept the bulk. The industry was inching toward the smartphone by adding power, color, applications, and better networks. It was not yet solving the emotional problem of mainstream desire. Microsoft brings the desktop's shadow Microsoft entered the same period with a very different inheritance. Its mobile strategy was tied to Windows CE, Pocket PC, Outlook, Office, synchronization, and the assumption that the mobile device would remain part of a Windows-centered personal computing world. This was not a foolish assumption. For many users, the PC was the hub. Email lived in Outlook. Documents lived in Office. Calendars and contacts lived on the desktop or the corporate server. A Pocket PC device that synchronized with a Windows machine could feel like an extension of the environment businesses already used. Microsoft did not need to invent personal information management; it already had a vast installed base of people managing work through Microsoft software. The challenge was that the pocket did not behave like the desktop. The mobile future would eventually include many Microsoft-like ideas: synchronization, messaging, enterprise support, media playback, productivity apps, remote access, and platform licensing. The Treo had a rechargeable battery rated for about 2.5 hours of talk time and 60 hours of standby. It had a clear cover to protect the touchscreen. Users could check calendar and address book information with the cover closed, make calls with a speakerphone or headset, use a jog dial for one-handed tasks, and browse the web with Handspring's Blazer browser. That paragraph contains a whole transitional moment. The Treo wanted to be a phone, pager, PDA, and web device, but the network still inserted pauses. It could browse, but the web still had to be adapted to the screen and connection. It could carry Palm's familiar applications, but it had to become more phone-like without losing the organizer's utility. It could have a keyboard or Graffiti because even Handspring had not fully decided which input model would define the future. The Treo also shows how much convergence was shaped by habit. Palm users already knew Date Book, Address Book, To Do, Memo Pad, HotSync, stylus taps, and the idea of carrying personal information. Cell-phone users, by contrast, had to be persuaded that they wanted PDA functions at all. That is why the Treo's design had to balance identities. Too PDA-like, and it would be too bulky or awkward as a phone. Too phone-like, and it would disappoint Palm loyalists who wanted the organizer to remain central. A good Treo had to be held to the ear, thumbed for messages, tapped with a stylus, synchronized with a PC, and tucked away without feeling absurd. The early models were promising but imperfect. It did not solve the future in one gesture. It evolved through the awkwardness. It made room for the thumb keyboard, the always-carried organizer, and the idea that a smartphone could be a daily business and personal device rather than only a corporate email machine or a miniature laptop. If the Nokia Communicator was the phone opening into a computer, the Treo was the organizer learning to talk. Why they still felt specialist From a distance, the Communicator era can look like a parade of almost-smartphones that simply needed better technology. That is partly true. Batteries improved. Processors improved. Displays improved. Wireless data improved. Component costs fell. But the deeper reason these devices remained specialist tools was not only that they were technically early. It was that their central use cases were still narrow, expensive, or socially uncertain. The Nokia Communicator made sense if mobile access to email, fax, contacts, and documents was valuable enough to justify a large, costly device. That described real people, but not everyone. The Kyocera 6035 made sense if a Palm-powered phone was worth the bulk and price. The R380 made sense if one wanted a phone-like PDA, but it did not offer the open software expansion that later users would expect. Pocket PC phones made sense if the user lived in a Windows and Outlook world and accepted the stylus-driven behavior of Microsoft's mobile devices. The Treo made sense if one wanted Palm's organizer, messaging, and phone functions in one compact package, and if one was willing to live inside the network limitations of the early 2000s. Each product had a believable customer. None had yet become the default shape of a phone. That is the difference between an early smartphone and a mature category. A mature category lets ordinary buyers choose among brands, prices, sizes, and styles without first deciding whether the whole idea is for them. In the Communicator era, the buyer was still choosing the idea. Do I want my phone to be a computer?

Do I want my organizer to be a phone? Do I want to type with thumbs? Do I want a stylus? Do I need email enough to carry this? Will the network work? Will my carrier sell it? A small phone for calls. A Palm for calendar and contacts. A BlackBerry for email. A laptop for real work. Perhaps a pager, depending on profession. The converged device had to beat each specialized object at enough of its own game. Most did not. There was also a cultural problem. The phone was becoming more personal and more social, but many early smartphones looked corporate, awkward, or technical. The broader mobile phone market was moving toward smaller, stylish, expressive devices. In Finland, Nokia phones were becoming extensions of the hand. In Europe and Asia, SMS culture was growing. In the United States, flip phones and smaller handsets carried their own appeal. The Communicator-class device could feel like a brilliant answer to a question that only a subset of users were asking. The future smartphone would need to be useful enough for professionals, but desirable enough for everyone else. The Communicator era mostly solved the first half. What they got right It is easy to look at these devices and notice the bulk, the slow networks, the styluses, the hinges, the tiny keyboards, the monochrome screens, the odd software, and the uncertain category labels. But they got many of the essentials right. They understood that the contact list and the phone call belonged together. They understood that email would matter on the move. They understood that a mobile device needed an operating system, not just embedded phone firmware.

They understood that third-party software would matter, even if they did not yet solve distribution. They understood that the browser, however primitive, would become part of the phone. They understood that the user might want to write more than a phone keypad comfortably allowed. They understood that the mobile device would become a carrier of identity, work, schedules, messages, and documents. They understood that the phone was no longer only a voice terminal. What they did not yet have was the right balance. The Nokia Communicator had too much computer to become an ordinary phone. The R380 had a phone-like body but too closed a software model. The Palm-phone hybrids had a familiar PDA interface but awkward physical compromises. Microsoft's Pocket PC phones had rich software but often carried too much desktop thinking. The Treo moved closer by making the device smaller, more phone-like, and more thumb-friendly, but even the Treo remained most natural to people who already wanted a PDA-phone. The industry was learning the ingredients, but not the recipe. The wait for urgency The next major breakthrough would not come from making the general-purpose smartphone better. It would come from making one mobile use case feel irresistible. That use case was email. The devices in this chapter could handle email in various ways. Nokia Communicators could send and receive it. Palm-phone hybrids could retrieve it. Treos would make it more practical. Pocket PC devices could synchronize with Outlook. But email on many of these devices still felt like a feature among features. Useful, yes. Sometimes impressive. Often slow, manual, or dependent on network conditions and configuration. BlackBerry made email feel alive. That is why the next chapter shifts away from the general communicator and toward a narrower device that, at first, did not need to be a full phone or a rich mobile computer. Research In Motion would not win the early 2000s by building the broadest pocket office. It would win by making the inbox urgent, portable, secure, and almost addictive. Its early genius was not convergence in the abstract. It was focus under pressure.

Chapter 6 BlackBerry and the Machine That Made Email Urgent At Logan Airport in Boston, not long after September 11, a state trooper could stand in the flow of passengers and carry something that looked almost too small for the job it had been given. It was not a laptop. It was not a police radio. It was not a rugged terminal bolted inside a cruiser. It was a pager-sized BlackBerry, the sort of thing more naturally associated with Wall Street briefcases than airport security checkpoints. In January 2002, Wired reported that Logan was testing BlackBerry devices as gateways to state and federal criminal databases. A trooper could send an encrypted query from the device and receive a response in less than a minute. He could check a suspicious person against a watch list without calling a dispatcher, walking back to a computer, or broadcasting details over a radio. The story was written in the language of post-9/11 security, but underneath it was something broader: a small device, clipped to the body, had become a portable line into institutional knowledge. The BlackBerry was not yet the modern smartphone in the way later users would understand that phrase. It was not a glass rectangle. It was not a camera, a music player, a game machine, a social-media portal, or a store for thousands of apps. It was not trying to delight teenagers with ringtones or make the web beautiful in the hand. It had a small screen, a physical keyboard, a trackwheel, an earpiece only in later models, and a practical seriousness that made it look less like a consumer electronics object than a tool someone had been issued. But it did one thing with unnerving clarity. It made messages arrive. That sounds ordinary now, almost too ordinary to deserve a chapter. In the age of push notifications, instant messaging, group chats, app alerts, Slack pings, calendar nudges, bank warnings, shipping updates, social-media likes, emergency notices, and promotional spam, the arrival of a message is no longer miraculous. It is often the opposite. It is fatigue. It is interruption. It is the glowing debt of attention that accumulates all day. BlackBerry belongs to the earlier moment when message arrival still felt like power. Before the BlackBerry, mobile email existed in fragments. A Nokia Communicator could send and receive email. A Palm could sync messages or connect through add-ons. A Pocket PC could cooperate with Outlook. Two-way pagers could send text. Laptops could dial in. Phones could use WAP or carrier services. But email still often felt like something one checked,

Research In Motion made email feel as though it had left the office and started following the person. That was the breakthrough. Not the phone. Not the web. Not even the keyboard alone. The breakthrough was the felt immediacy of the inbox. The BlackBerry turned email from a place into a current. It made work continuous. And for the people whose professional lives were built around responsiveness — executives, lawyers, bankers, consultants, salespeople, politicians, journalists, government officials, emergency managers, and corporate IT departments — that current became hard to step out of. The smartphone would later become a device that could do almost anything. BlackBerry first became the device that made one thing impossible to ignore. It came from Waterloo, Ontario, a university city with engineering talent, Canadian telecommunications context, and a culture more likely to admire technical persistence than theatrical product launches. The company was founded in 1984 by Mike Lazaridis and Douglas Fregin, with Lazaridis leaving the University of Waterloo after winning a General Motors contract and using that opportunity, along with other funding, to build the company that would eventually become synonymous with BlackBerry. The early RIM was not a phone company in the Nokia sense, and it was not a personal- computer company in the Apple or Microsoft sense. It was a wireless data company. That distinction matters because BlackBerry’s later success did not begin with a desire to make a glamorous handset. It began with the less glamorous problem of moving information over wireless networks in small packets, reliably enough that businesses would trust it. That history gave RIM a particular kind of patience. Voice telephony was not the center of the company’s imagination at first. Wireless data was. In the late 1980s and 1990s, RIM worked on connectivity products for Mobitex, a packet-switched wireless data network, and on gateways, modems, and data devices long before the BlackBerry name became famous.

The office leaves the office The BlackBerry changed work because it changed the boundaries of work in a way that felt voluntary before it felt compulsory. For many users, the first experience was liberation. The device meant they no longer had to return to a desk to know what was happening. Still, convergence kept pressing. If the BlackBerry was already on the belt, already in the hand, already tied to the user’s work identity, why should voice remain separate? The BlackBerry 5810, released in 2002, was one of the first BlackBerry models to integrate mobile phone capability, though it required a headset to make calls. The 6000-series devices that followed in 2003, including the 6210, moved closer to the familiar BlackBerry phone form, with integrated voice capability and the email-centric keyboard still at the center.

Page 99

This evolution mattered because it changed BlackBerry from an email companion into a fuller mobile communications device. Yet even as voice arrived, email remained the soul of the product. That is why BlackBerry’s phone features felt different from those of Nokia, Motorola, or Ericsson. It was not a phone that had learned email. It was an email machine that had learned to speak. That difference shaped the user experience. On many phones, messaging was an add-on to calling. On BlackBerry, calling was an add-on to messaging. The keyboard came first. The inbox came first. The trackwheel and message list came first. The device’s posture remained textual even after it became vocal. This gave BlackBerry a deep advantage in professional markets, where the written message often mattered more than the call. Email created records. Email crossed time zones. Email allowed measured replies. Email could be copied, forwarded, archived, searched, and escalated. Phone calls were intimate and immediate, but they could interrupt too much. BlackBerry let users live in the space between real-time conversation and delayed correspondence. It made asynchronous communication feel urgent without making it quite as intrusive as a phone call. That was a new kind of power. Security, secrecy, and the portable archive The very thing that made BlackBerry valuable also made it risky. By pushing corporate email into pockets, BlackBerry turned the handheld into a portable archive of sensitive life. This was not merely theoretical. In 2003, Wired reported the story of a Seattle computer consultant who bought a used BlackBerry on eBay for $15.50 and discovered more than 200 internal Morgan Stanley emails and a database of more than 1,000 names, titles, email addresses, and phone numbers for executives. The previous owner, a former vice president at Morgan Stanley, said he had assumed the data was gone after removing the battery; it was not. The story is striking not because BlackBerry uniquely caused careless data handling, but because it revealed what a mobile device had become. It was no longer a simple endpoint. It held organizational memory. It duplicated parts of the office. It carried private correspondence, client information, employee directories, and traces of decisions that might never have left the building in an earlier era. This was the enterprise bargain in its starkest form. BlackBerry gave workers access to corporate knowledge anywhere. That meant corporate knowledge could also be lost anywhere, sold anywhere, stolen anywhere, or forgotten in a taxi. The more useful the device became, the more dangerous mishandling it became.

Page 100

RIM’s answer, and the enterprise’s answer, was more management: policies, passwords, remote wipe, server control, training, encryption, provisioning, and IT process. But the broader lesson reached beyond BlackBerry. Once data moved into pockets, corporate security could no longer be built around the walls of the office. The perimeter had gone mobile. Later smartphones would make this problem far bigger. They would carry personal photos, work documents, location trails, health data, banking credentials, private messages, and app tokens. But BlackBerry helped introduce the enterprise version of the problem. The pocket was no longer just where a professional carried a phone. It was where the company’s information traveled. The network in the middle A BlackBerry felt direct. Email arrived on the device. The user replied. The message moved. But the simplicity on the surface depended on infrastructure in the middle. That infrastructure was one reason BlackBerry worked so well, and also one reason it created strange dependencies. RIM’s system routed traffic through its own infrastructure, which helped with compression, delivery, enterprise integration, and the distinctive push experience. For users and companies, this could feel like reliability. For RIM, it was a powerful strategic position: BlackBerry was not just selling hardware; it was operating part of the service layer. But centrality has a cost. When the system failed, millions of users felt it together. In April 2007, Wired reported that RIM’s BlackBerry service suffered a major outage beginning around 8 p.m. Eastern, delaying or stopping email for users and, in some reports, web browsing as well. RIM’s recorded service message said technicians had tried to reset the network, but a large backlog created problems as service returned. The outage appeared to affect much of North America, while England was reportedly unaffected. The outage was news because BlackBerry had become infrastructure. A toy can fail quietly. A personal gadget can annoy its owner. But when BlackBerry failed, the interruption resembled a professional weather event. Executives, lawyers, finance workers, government officials, and corporate users had built work rhythms around the assumption that messages would arrive. When they did not, the absence itself became visible. The following year, Wired reported another BlackBerry email-server malfunction that affected email, registration, and internet browsing services, though voice services were not affected; the article noted that it was the third major outage for the company in the previous year and invoked users’ dependence on their “daily dose” of CrackBerry.

Outages like this revealed a paradox. BlackBerry users loved the system because it was dependable enough to reorganize their habits. The system became frightening when those habits had nowhere else to go. The same infrastructure that made the device work so seamlessly also concentrated failure. This, too, foreshadows the smartphone era. When later platforms centralized app stores, cloud backups, messaging services, identity systems, and notification infrastructure, the convenience was immense. So was the dependency. BlackBerry taught professionals what it felt like when the pocket became a service and the service went dark. Growth by necessity BlackBerry’s rise was not accidental, and it was not merely cultural. The numbers grew because the product solved a concrete problem with unusual focus. By the early 2000s, BlackBerry’s subscriber base was expanding rapidly. Later company- history summaries report active BlackBerry subscribers growing from 25,000 in 2000 to 165,000 in 2001, 321,000 in 2002, 534,000 by March 2003, more than one million by February 2004, and 2.51 million by February 2005. By March 2007, on the eve of the iPhone’s arrival, BlackBerry had about eight million active subscribers; by early 2008, about fourteen million. The growth also showed up in device sales and category rankings. In 2007, Wired reported Gartner data saying 17.7 million handheld computers shipped globally in 2006; RIM led the category in the article’s framing, with sales climbing 10 percent to 3.5 million units, while Palm fell and HP declined. The methodology made distinctions between PDAs and smartphones that already looked awkward, but that awkwardness itself is instructive. BlackBerry was forcing analysts to decide what category it belonged to. Was it a PDA? A pager? A phone? A smartphone? An enterprise email terminal? A mobile computer? A service? The answer changed depending on the model and the moment. Early BlackBerrys looked like two-way pagers. The 957 looked like a wireless email device. The 5810 and 6210 moved into phone territory. Later Curves, Pearls, Bolds, and other models would become more obviously smartphone-like. But across all of them, the essential business remained the same: BlackBerry sold mobile urgency. That urgency scaled especially well in organizations. One BlackBerry in a company could be an executive perk. Ten could be a pilot program. Fifty could be a policy. Hundreds could change expectations. Once a manager responded from a cab, another manager had to respond from a train. Once a lawyer replied at midnight, a client learned midnight replies were possible. Once a team could coordinate by BlackBerry during travel, the old excuse of being away from email weakened.

Adoption did not only reflect demand. It created demand. The more reachable people became, the more reachability became expected. The government machine BlackBerry also became deeply associated with government, law, and emergency communication, not only because it offered mobile email but because it offered a particular mixture of portability, security, and institutional control. The Logan Airport example is one glimpse. A small device could be used by law enforcement to query databases quickly and securely in the field. That kind of use made the BlackBerry feel less like a convenience and more like an operational tool. The patent litigation around NTP later revealed the extent of government dependence more dramatically. In 2006, as RIM fought the possibility of a U.S. service shutdown in its patent battle with NTP, reports noted concerns among government users and emergency communicators. Wired’s coverage of the Supreme Court declining to hear RIM’s appeal described the possibility of an injunction that could block BlackBerry service for many U.S. users, while an attorney for RIM argued that federal law would prevent cutting off government and emergency users who relied on the devices. This was not the kind of legal risk a normal consumer gadget created. A shutdown would not merely inconvenience gadget enthusiasts; it threatened to disrupt the communications habits of corporations and government agencies. RIM eventually settled the NTP dispute in March 2006, agreeing to pay $612.5 million to resolve the litigation. The NTP case belongs partly to patent history, and partly to BlackBerry history because it showed how essential the device had become. The more indispensable the service, the more leverage a legal threat had. If a company’s product can be shut down without broader anxiety, it is not yet infrastructure. BlackBerry had crossed that line. The company had not merely sold devices. It had inserted itself into the daily operation of institutions. Why people loved it To understand BlackBerry’s later failure, one must first understand the affection. People did not love BlackBerry because it had the best screen, the most beautiful interface, the richest browser, the most playful design, or the broadest consumer appeal. They loved it because, for a certain kind of life, it worked at the exact point of pressure. The pressure was not abstract productivity. It was the tension between movement and responsibility. A person had to leave the office but remain accountable to the office. A person

Page 103

had to travel but remain in the loop. A person had to attend meetings but keep one eye on incoming information. A person had to be unreachable enough to move, but reachable enough to matter. BlackBerry made that possible. It also made the user feel competent. This is important. The device’s small screen and keyboard could look ridiculous to outsiders, but to a practiced user the BlackBerry was fast. The trackwheel gave lists a rhythm. The keyboard made replies real. The inbox could be processed quickly. The battery life on early email devices was often better suited to day-long professional use than later multimedia smartphones. The device could live in a holster, wake instantly, and do its job without the distractions of a richer computer. For some users, that focus became part of the romance. The BlackBerry did not ask them to browse a beautiful web or manage an entertainment library. It made them sharper at work. In a corporate culture that rewarded responsiveness, that felt like power. There was also a social pride in mastering the device. BlackBerry users could type quickly with their thumbs in a way nonusers found surprising. They could answer emails while walking. They could handle matters before returning to the desk. They could be “on” in a way that seemed modern, important, and slightly dangerous. The device was not beautiful in the way the iPod would be beautiful. It was beautiful in the way a good tool is beautiful: because repeated use deepens attachment. A hammer is not loved for its interface. A fountain pen is not loved for its feature list. A BlackBerry was loved because the user’s hand learned it. Why the love became a trap The same qualities that made BlackBerry beloved also narrowed its imagination. The company had found an extraordinary fit between professional email, physical keyboard, enterprise infrastructure, carrier distribution, and user identity. That fit made BlackBerry powerful. It also made it hard to see which parts of the product were permanent and which were historically temporary. The keyboard felt permanent to users who could not imagine writing serious email on glass. The enterprise server felt permanent to companies that trusted BlackBerry more than consumer platforms. The inbox felt permanent because email was the center of professional life.

Page 104

The corporate buyer felt permanent because that was where BlackBerry had won. The device’s seriousness felt permanent because the smartphone had not yet become a mass consumer media object. Each of these assumptions was true enough to build a company around. Each would later become vulnerable. But that later story should not erase the earlier victory. BlackBerry did not fail because it misunderstood mobile from the beginning. It succeeded because it understood one part of mobile better than anyone else. It saw that the professional inbox could move to the pocket, and it built the device, keyboard, server, network strategy, and cultural identity around that fact. The trap was not ignorance. The trap was success. This is why BlackBerry should not be treated as a comic prelude to the iPhone, or as a company that simply missed touchscreens. For years, it gave users exactly what they wanted. It made email immediate. It made mobile work practical. It made corporate IT comfortable with pockets full of data. It made the physical keyboard iconic. It made reachability professional, and then addictive, and then expected. That is a major achievement. It is also the beginning of a problem no company in this book entirely solves: when a product makes users more available, who owns the time that becomes available? BlackBerry did not ask that question in philosophical terms. It asked whether the message could arrive. It could. The pocket learns urgency By the time BlackBerry was entrenched in offices, law firms, banks, newsrooms, government agencies, and executive culture, the broader phone world was moving in a different direction. Outside the BlackBerry’s professional bubble, millions of people were learning another kind of mobile communication. They were texting on keypads. They were choosing ringtones. They were taking fuzzy camera-phone pictures. They were buying fashion phones, prepaid phones, Nokia candy bars, Motorola clamshells, Sony Ericsson camera phones, Samsung sliders, and LG handsets. In Japan, mobile internet services had already become woven into everyday life in ways the U.S. market had not matched. In Europe and Asia, SMS

culture gave ordinary users a form of mobile written communication that did not depend on corporate email. BlackBerry made email urgent for professionals. Feature phones made mobile communication social, stylish, cheap, and global. Those were different paths toward the same future. One path began with work; the other with daily life. One path began in the enterprise server; the other in carrier stores, youth culture, prepaid plans, camera novelties, text messages, and the strange early economies of ringtones and wallpapers. The smartphone that eventually swallowed the world would inherit both paths. It would need BlackBerry’s immediacy, Nokia’s scale, Japan’s mobile internet habits, Motorola’s fashion instinct, Palm’s organizer discipline, the Treo’s convergence experiments, the iPod’s emotional design, and eventually Apple and Google’s platform strategies. But before the iPhone could reframe the phone, the phone itself had to become culturally huge. Not smart in the BlackBerry sense. Not smart in the Nokia Communicator sense. Not smart in the Palm sense. Just everywhere. Chapter 7 Feature Phones, Fashion Phones, and the Mobile Internet That Wasn’t Quite the Internet The phone on the table did not look like a computer. That was part of its charm. It was small enough to disappear in a fist, smooth enough to slide into the front pocket of a pair of jeans, and simple enough that a person could understand its basic promise before the clerk at the carrier store finished talking. It might have been a Nokia candy bar with a greenish monochrome screen, rubbery keys, and a battery that seemed to last forever. It might have been a Motorola clamshell that opened with a snap and closed with a little drama. It might have had a stubby antenna, or no visible antenna at all. It might have been silver, blue, red, translucent, rounded, angular, or wrapped in a replaceable plastic cover bought from a kiosk in a mall. It might have been free with a contract, or nearly free, or strangely expensive because it was thin, metallic, and beautiful. To call it “dumb” would be wrong. The feature phone was full of intelligence, but its intelligence lived inside limits. It could remember numbers. It could wake you in the morning. It could send text messages. It could play games. It could make a teenager feel private in a crowded house. It could flash, vibrate, beep, sing, and display a tiny picture. It could take a photograph, badly at first, but with enough immediacy that quality almost did not matter. It could show stock prices, weather, horoscopes, sports scores, news headlines, train schedules, and little menu pages from carrier portals. In Japan, it could do much more than that: email, games, ticketing, payments, photos, and a kind of mobile internet that made much of the rest of the world’s “wireless web” look like a bad joke. NTT DoCoMo’s i-mode, launched in February 1999, had more than 25 million subscribers by 2001, while WAP services in the U.S. and Europe were widely criticized for being slow, cramped, and frustrating. But a feature phone usually did not behave like a general-purpose computer. That was why people trusted it. It was a phone first. The calendar, the game, the ringtone, the camera, the browser, and the messages sat behind the basic fact of reachability. The device’s job was to be ready. A phone could not ask for the patience people gave to PCs. It could not crash in public and expect forgiveness. It could not spend the day plugged into a wall. It had to survive pockets, purses, backpacks, school desks, coffee tables, dashboards, rain, dust, drops, and the routine neglect of ordinary life. In the years before the iPhone, this was the world most people knew. Not Newton. Not General Magic. Not a Nokia Communicator opened like a tiny laptop. Not a BlackBerry vibrating with corporate email. Those mattered deeply, but they were not the whole phone

culture. For hundreds of millions of people, mobile life was built around simpler objects: keypads, small screens, SMS, ringtones, prepaid minutes, SIM cards, carrier stores, repair shops, chargers borrowed from friends, and the satisfying certainty that the call would go through. The smartphone would eventually grow out of this world as much as it grew out of the PDA or the laptop. The future did not arrive only from professional email and pocket organizers. It also arrived from teenagers learning to type without looking, from office workers choosing polyphonic tones, from Japanese commuters checking i-mode pages on trains, from shoppers picking the thinnest clamshell in the store, from children playing Snake, from couples sending goodnight texts, from camera phones turning everyday moments into images, and from carriers discovering that the phone could be a storefront. This was the age when the mobile phone became culturally huge before the smartphone became technically dominant. It was the age when the phone stopped being merely useful and became personal. The extension of the hand In Finland, the slang word kännykkä meant something close to “extension of the hand.” Wired used the phrase in a 1999 profile of Nokia, and it is hard to find a better description of what the mobile phone was becoming before the smartphone era. The word captured a shift that no specification sheet could fully explain. The phone was no longer only a communications device. It was a bodily habit. It belonged to the hand, and, by extension, to the person. Nokia understood that shift earlier and more completely than most of its rivals. By the late 1990s, the company had reinvented itself around digital phones and networks, and its rise was tied to GSM, the European standard that made roaming, SMS, and a unified mobile market more practical than the fragmented systems that preceded it. In July 1991, the first call on a commercial GSM network was placed in Finland on a Nokia phone, and by the end of the decade Nokia had moved past Motorola to become the world’s leading mobile-phone maker. The company’s genius in that period was not simply engineering, though the engineering mattered. It was a feel for the phone as an everyday object. Nokia made devices that were durable, friendly, readable, and often just playful enough. They were not ashamed to be appliances. They did not require the user to pretend to be a computer enthusiast. They had menus that millions learned, keypads that could be operated by memory, and a design language that made phones feel less like elite equipment and more like companions.

Page 109

A Nokia phone could be conservative, businesslike, youthful, rugged, cheap, luxurious, or experimental. The company seemed to understand that the mobile phone was not one market but many. A phone for a construction worker, a schoolgirl, a banker, a rural first-time buyer, a fashion-conscious urban user, and a traveling executive could not all be the same object. Nokia’s product line sprawled, sometimes confusingly, but the sprawl reflected a real insight: once phones became personal, they had to express different kinds of personhood. The simplest models were often the most consequential. The Nokia 1100, released in 2003, became one of the best-selling phones ever, with sales commonly reported above 250 million units. It had a small monochrome screen, a flashlight, a user-replaceable battery, a simple keypad, and a price and feature set suited to users who needed calls, texts, alarms, and reliability more than glamour. That little flashlight deserves more respect than it usually gets. In histories of mobile computing, people tend to focus on processors, operating systems, app stores, cameras, and browsers. But for many users in developing markets, a built-in flashlight was not a gimmick. It was a feature that understood the conditions of use: dark streets, power outages, shared rooms, rural areas, night travel. It was a reminder that “mobile innovation” was not always about making the phone more computer-like. Sometimes it was about making the phone more useful in the world where it would actually live. The 1100 did not point toward the iPhone in any obvious visual way. It had no touchscreen. It had no app store. It was not a platform in the later smartphone sense. But it helped make the mobile phone universal. It brought the handset to people who did not need a PDA, did not want a laptop, and might never have carried a BlackBerry. The smartphone would later arrive in many of those same hands, but the habit of carrying a phone had already been created. That is one of the deepest reasons feature phones matter. They normalized possession. Before smartphones could become indispensable, phones had to become ordinary. Learning to text The first SMS message was not romantic in the way later texting would become. In December 1992, Neil Papworth sent “Merry Christmas” from a computer to a Vodafone executive’s Orbitel handset in the United Kingdom. The phone could receive the text but not reply. It was a technical test, not the birth of a social universe in anyone’s mind. But SMS had the strange advantage of underestimation. It was small, limited, and, at first, peripheral. The 160-character constraint did not look like a revolution. It looked like a control-channel feature, a modest standard inside the GSM world. Yet constraints are often

Page 110

where culture begins. A message short enough to fit on a tiny screen could be sent without making a call. It could be private in public. It could be asynchronous. It could be cheap enough, depending on the market and plan, to support casual repetition. It could carry flirtation, coordination, apology, gossip, warning, boredom, jokes, and a hundred small negotiations too trivial or too delicate for voice. Voice calls require a shared moment. Texts loosen the moment. One person sends, the other replies later, and the conversation survives interruption. That was not merely a technical feature. It changed the etiquette of everyday life. European teenagers helped reveal what SMS could become. By 2002, Wired was already writing about “generation text,” text-message slang, and the cultural worry that SMS might be replacing richer forms of human communication. In the United Kingdom alone, another Wired report from that period cited 45 million SMS messages being sent daily. Adults often treated this as linguistic decline. Teenagers treated it as a medium. The keypad shaped the language. To type “c” on a basic phone, one might press the 2 key three times. To type “s,” the 7 key four times. Predictive systems like T9 helped, but even without them, users developed speed, rhythm, and compression. Words lost vowels. Phrases became codes. Emotions became abbreviations and punctuation. The phone did not simply transmit language; it pushed language through the mechanics of the keypad and the economics of the message. This was one of the first great mass lessons in mobile interface adaptation. People will learn awkward input methods if the social reward is strong enough. Graffiti had proved something similar for Palm users, but SMS did it at a much larger cultural scale. Teenagers and ordinary phone owners learned to write on devices that had never been designed primarily for prose because the need to send small private messages was stronger than the inconvenience. This matters because later touchscreen keyboards did not replace nothing. They replaced a deeply learned culture of keypad text. When skeptics in 2007 mocked the iPhone’s lack of a physical keyboard, they were not merely defending corporate BlackBerry habits. They were also defending a broader assumption: serious mobile text required physical keys. The feature-phone era had trained billions of thumbs. The sound of belonging Ringtones were the first mobile app economy that embarrassed everyone and made money anyway.

Before phones could carry large music libraries, before streaming, before app stores, before social platforms discovered every possible way to monetize attention, the ringtone turned the phone into a tiny public performance. A call came in, and the device announced not only that someone wanted you, but something about who you were, or at least who you wanted to appear to be. The default ring was institutional. A personalized ringtone was social. It could be a television theme, a pop song, a joke, a sound effect, a classical melody, a dance track, or, later, a snippet of the original recording. It made the phone less like equipment and more like a possession. In a world where many handsets looked similar, the sound individualized the object. The money was startling. The ringtone business was strange because it sold scarcity inside a technology that would soon make audio abundant. A polyphonic ringtone was not really a song in the sense of a recording. It was a little arrangement, a synthesized approximation, a melody made by the phone itself. The New Yorker, writing in 2005, described polyphonic ringtones as tiny packets of code that made the phone play like a music box, and noted that master tones — compressed snippets of actual recordings — were already taking over large parts of the U.S. ringtone market as newer phones improved. This was the phone as identity surface. First the cover. Then the ringtone. Then the wallpaper. Then the camera photo. Then the contact photo. Then the theme. Then the app icon. Then the lock screen. The smartphone would later make customization broader and more visual, but the impulse was already alive in feature phones. People wanted the phone to feel like theirs. The ringtone also taught carriers and content companies a lesson they would never forget: small digital goods could be billed through the phone. The carrier relationship mattered because the carrier controlled billing, distribution, portals, menus, short codes, and access to customers. The ringtone was not just a novelty. It was a proof of mobile commerce. People would pay for little pieces of personality if the purchase was easy enough and the charge could appear on the phone bill. The future app store was not born from ringtones, but the ringtone economy showed how mobile content could become both intimate and lucrative.

The store inside the carrier For much of the feature-phone era, the carrier was not only the company that provided the network. It was the shopkeeper of the mobile experience. This was especially true in the United States, where phones were often sold through carrier stores, locked to networks, subsidized through contracts, and loaded with carrier- approved menus, services, and branding. The carrier controlled not only pricing but possibility. Which phone could you buy? Which features would work? Which portal did the browser open? Which ringtones, games, wallpapers, and messaging services were promoted? Which data plan made sense? Which services cost extra? The phone was a device, but it was also the front end of a billing relationship. To understand why the iPhone’s later negotiation with AT&T/Cingular mattered, one has to understand this older world. Carriers had grown accustomed to shaping the customer’s mobile life. Handset makers wanted shelf space, subsidies, certification, and promotion. Content providers wanted placement inside the carrier deck. Users might think they were choosing a phone, but they were often entering a managed environment. The early mobile internet was therefore not simply a technical failure. It was a business model with a user interface. WAP, the Wireless Application Protocol, was supposed to bring web-like content to phones. In practice, many users found it slow, cramped, and difficult. Wired’s 2001 report on WAP’s reputation summarized the complaints: cumbersome keypad input, small screens, slow data transmission, and a shortage of compelling WML content. Another Wired item from 2002 noted that American consumers had largely rejected early WAP experiences, with critics using the slogan “WAP is crap.” The phrase was cruel, but it captured the disappointment of a generation of users who had been promised the internet and given something that felt like a bad menu system. Pages loaded slowly. Text was chopped into fragments. Links were hard to select. Forms were miserable to fill out. Content was often shallow. The user could feel the gap between the desktop web and the phone version at every step. The problem was not that phones could not access information. They could. The problem was that the experience rarely felt like the internet people were learning to love on PCs. It felt like a carrier-controlled imitation of the internet, squeezed through small screens and awkward input, priced in ways that made curiosity risky. That distinction is essential. The mobile internet existed before smartphones, but in many markets it did not yet feel open, abundant, or natural. It felt managed. It felt like a service. It felt like something one used for a purpose and then escaped from.

The iPhone’s later “real internet” framing would work because so many people had experienced the unreal one. Japan’s different future Japan complicated every Western assumption about mobile phones. By the early 2000s, if one looked only at the United States or much of Europe, it was tempting to conclude that mobile internet services were premature or inherently disappointing. Then one looked at Japan and saw millions of people using phones for email, games, information, entertainment, and commerce long before the iPhone. DoCoMo’s i-mode was the central example. Introduced in February 1999 with modest expectations, i-mode grew rapidly, passing 25 million subscribers by 2001. Wired reported that new users were joining at about 43,000 per day at that point, and that the service’s advertisements did not even emphasize the word “internet.” The “i” was framed as information, tied visually to information booths, and the service was marketed as useful mobile content rather than a tiny desktop web. That marketing choice mattered. Many Western carriers and technology companies sold WAP as if it were a mobile version of the web. It was then judged by web standards and found wanting. i-mode did not ask users to imagine a full browser squeezed into a phone. It offered things people could actually do on the device: email, weather, games, news, transit information, restaurant listings, stocks, and paid content. It matched the service to the device instead of asking the device to imitate the PC. Its business model also mattered. DoCoMo’s packet-switched network meant users were charged by the amount of data sent and received rather than the time they were connected. Wired also reported that by 2001, i-mode had about 1,800 official offerings monitored by DoCoMo and tens of thousands of unofficial sites reachable by URL; official sites could charge small monthly fees, often in the ¥100 to ¥300 range, with DoCoMo collecting payment for a 9 percent fee. Games in the margins Games entered the phone quietly, almost accidentally at first. Snake did not need a color screen, a gamepad, or a broadband connection. They had already paid for weather, sports, games, stock quotes, maps, and email. They had already learned that the phone could be a portal. The frustration of feature-phone internet services was not a sign that mobile data was unwanted. It was a sign that the right interface and business model had not yet arrived in most markets. The smartphone did not create mobile curiosity. It relieved some of its pain. The phones people actually loved It is easy, after the smartphone, to treat feature phones as transitional clutter. For letting a child call home. For giving a teenager privacy. For carrying Snake. For making ringtones absurdly personal. For glowing in the dark. For snapping shut at the end of a call. Part III The iPod Bridge Chapter 8 The iPod as Smartphone Prequel The room was small enough that the product could not hide behind spectacle. This was not Moscone West, not the kind of stage where a new Apple device could be introduced to thousands of people under theatrical lights, with the crowd already primed to cheer before the first slide appeared. On October 23, 2001, Apple gathered reporters and guests at its campus in Cupertino and introduced something that was, at least in public, more modest than the revolutions the company had once promised. It was not a new Macintosh. It was not an operating system. It was not a tablet, not a phone, not a networked communicator, not the long-awaited future of computing. It was a music player. That was what made the moment easy to underestimate. The phrase Steve Jobs used that day - "1,000 songs in your pocket" - had the precision of great consumer product language because it did not require anyone to care about hard-drive geometry, audio codecs, FireWire bandwidth, lithium-polymer chemistry, file management, or Apple’s larger strategy. Diamond Multimedia’s Rio and other MP3 players had already existed. Creative had already made hard-drive-based players. General Magic had promised a pocket networked life and arrived too early. Palm had made the organizer simple enough to use every day. BlackBerry had made email urgent. Nokia and Motorola had made phones ordinary, expressive, and global. The iPod gave Apple a different lesson: how to make a small object feel like a private world, how to tie that object to desktop software, how to make content portable without making the user think too much about transfer, and how to sell a device not merely as useful, but as emotionally desirable. Before the iPhone could become the phone that swallowed the iPod, the iPod had to become the object that taught Apple how pocket desire worked. Before the white rectangle At the turn of the millennium, digital music was both everywhere and nowhere. On college campuses, in bedrooms, in offices, and in the spaces between legitimate commerce and mass infringement, songs were moving through computers in ways the recording industry could not control. Napster had made file-sharing into a cultural event. The scroll wheel and the long list The iPod’s defining interface problem was not how to enter text, but how to move through abundance. Palm had been designed around the small facts of a person’s life: dates, contacts, tasks, notes. BlackBerry had been designed around the incoming stream of messages. The iPod was designed around a library that was suddenly too large for buttons. A portable CD player gave the user an album. A cassette player gave the user a side of tape. A laptop can appear in a cafe, a library, an airport, or a meeting, but it is often open only during a session. The iPod was public while being private. A person walking down the street with white earbuds was alone inside music and yet visibly part of a new habit. The ads did not show file management, FireWire, or even the face of the listener. They showed movement and possession. The message was not “this device has 5GB.” It was “this device changes the body in public.” Apple’s iPod advertising, especially the silhouette campaign, became one of the defining visual languages of the product line and helped detach the iPod from the nerdy reputation of MP3 players. Source: summaries of Apple advertising history and contemporaneous coverage. This was a different kind of mobile status than the car phone, the pager, the BlackBerry, or the RAZR. The car phone had said importance. The pager had said reachability. The BlackBerry had said professional necessity. The RAZR had said style. The iPod said interiority. It made a public display out of private attention. That psychological quality would matter later for smartphones. A smartphone is not only a communication device; it is also a way of being alone in public, of carrying a private world through shared space. Music was one of the first forms in which that became visually normal. Long before commuters looked down into feeds, they listened behind earbuds. The iPod helped make the portable private world socially acceptable. The phone industry had made people reachable. The iPod made them enclosed. The iPhone would do both. The store that made the device bigger The iPod did not begin with the iTunes Music Store. For its first year and a half, it was a device and software system without Apple’s own song marketplace. Users ripped CDs, copied existing files, and managed libraries in iTunes. That was powerful enough to build early momentum, but it left Apple dependent on a chaotic music ecosystem outside its control. The iTunes Music Store, launched on April 28, 2003, changed the scale of the system. Apple offered more than 200,000 songs from major labels, 99-cent downloads without subscription fees, 30-second previews, integration into iTunes 4, AAC encoding, and personal-use rights that included playing songs on up to three Macs, listening on unlimited iPods, and burning songs to CDs for personal use. Apple’s announcement framed the store not only as a commercial service but as a moral and practical compromise: users did not want to be treated like criminals, artists did not want music stolen, and Apple claimed to offer a

Page 129

workable solution for both. Source: Apple Newsroom, "Apple Launches the iTunes Music Store," April 28, 2003. The store was important because it made the iPod feel less like an accessory and more like a system. A standalone MP3 player depended on whatever music the user could find. The iPod plus iTunes plus the Music Store offered a complete route: discover, preview, buy, organize, sync, listen. Apple did not control the entire music universe, but it controlled enough of the experience to make legal digital music feel easier than the alternatives for many users. That was a remarkable achievement in a market where the industry had spent years trying to protect itself from users rather than designing something users would want. The 99-cent price mattered. It made a song feel purchasable without ceremony, less like buying an album and more like picking a track from a jukebox that followed you home. The absence of a subscription fee mattered, too. Apple’s model aligned with the way many listeners still thought about ownership: buy a song, keep the song, put it on the device. The rights were constrained by DRM, but compared with many legal alternatives of the time, the store felt permissive enough to be attractive. This store-and-device relationship would become central to Apple’s later platform strategy. The iTunes Music Store was not the App Store, but it taught Apple how to negotiate with content owners, manage digital rights, process small transactions, host a marketplace, control a catalog, integrate commerce into desktop software, and turn a device into an endpoint for paid digital goods. It taught the company that a hardware product could become more valuable when tied to a content platform, and that a content platform could become more powerful when tied to a beloved device. When Apple later launched the App Store for iPhone, the company was not inventing digital distribution from scratch. It had years of experience selling small digital objects to consumers and keeping the transaction inside Apple’s interface. Music was the rehearsal. At first, the iTunes Music Store was Mac-only, which limited its reach. The larger world still ran Windows. Apple’s October 2003 launch of iTunes for Windows therefore mattered almost as much as the original store launch. Apple announced that Windows users would get the same store, catalog, personal-use rights, and 99-cent pricing; the company said that users had purchased more than 13 million songs in the first six months and that the catalog would reach more than 400,000 songs by the end of October. Source: Apple Newsroom, "Apple Launches iTunes for Windows," October 16, 2003. This was Apple crossing a line it would cross again with the iPhone in a different form. The company loved integration, but it also knew when the market was too large to ignore. The iPod could not remain only a Mac accessory if it wanted to dominate portable music. The

Windows support did not dilute the iPod strategy; it expanded the universe in which the iPod could become normal. That was another lesson for the smartphone era: Apple could be vertically integrated without being completely insular. It could control the experience while reaching beyond its own traditional base. The line becomes a family A single successful product can create a category. A family of products can occupy it. The original iPod was white, hard-drive-based, relatively expensive, and aimed at people who wanted a large library. But music listeners were not all the same. Some wanted a smaller device. Some wanted color. Some wanted a lower price. Some wanted something for running. Some wanted fashion. Some wanted more storage. Some wanted a gift item. Some wanted the Apple device but not the $399 commitment. Apple’s answer was to make the iPod into a line. The iPod mini, introduced in 2004, was smaller, colorful, and less expensive than the full- size iPod, though not cheap enough to silence critics who wanted a more dramatic price drop. Wired reported strong early demand, including 100,000 pre-orders before retail release, for the $249, 4GB model that stored about 1,000 songs and came in colors that made the device feel less like a computer peripheral and more like a personal accessory. Source: Wired, "iPod Mini Shrinks, Goes Pink," February 2004. The mini’s importance is easy to miss because the later nano overshadowed it, but the mini showed that the iPod could be segmented by identity. Color mattered. Size mattered. The device no longer had to stand only for capacity. It could stand for taste. This was the same consumer-electronics logic that feature phones had already discovered with faceplates, clamshells, and fashion models. Apple applied it to portable media with a cleaner brand architecture. The iPod shuffle, introduced in January 2005, pushed in the opposite direction: less interface, lower price, flash memory, randomness as a feature. At $99 for the 512MB model, it was Apple’s entry into the lower end of the market, smaller than a pack of gum and designed around the idea that one did not always need to choose the song. Wired described it as part of a Macworld keynote alongside the Mac mini, another product aimed at expanding Apple’s reach downward without abandoning the brand’s sense of simplicity. Source: Wired, January 2005 Macworld coverage.

The shuffle was philosophically bold because it removed the screen. In the history of mobile devices, screens usually represented progress. More screen meant more control, more information, more visible power. Apple temporarily stepped away from that assumption. For a running playlist, a commute, a pocket clipped to clothing, or a user who wanted music without the ceremony of browsing, no screen could be a feature rather than a flaw. “Shuffle” turned limitation into personality. Then came the nano, replacing the mini in 2005 with a flash-based device so small and thin that it seemed to compress the iPod idea into jewelry. Apple’s later retrospective describes the original iPod mini as arriving in February 2004 at 3.6 ounces, and the second- generation nano, introduced in September 2006, as offering a thin design, bright color display, six colors, up to 24 hours of battery life, and up to 2,000 songs. Source: Apple Newsroom, "The music lives on," May 2022. The nano mattered because it showed Apple’s increasing ability to manage component transitions. Hard drives made the first iPod possible; flash memory made smaller, more durable, more colorful versions possible. The company was learning how to use storage technology not only as a specification, but as a product-shaping material. Storage determined thickness, weight, battery life, durability, and price. In the smartphone era, flash storage would become one of the invisible foundations of the device in everyone’s pocket. The iPod line helped train Apple to treat storage as an experience variable, not merely a capacity number. By 2005, the iPod with video brought another shift. Apple introduced a 30GB model for $299 and a 60GB model for $399, with a 2.5-inch screen and video playback, while the iTunes store expanded to include music videos and television shows. Wired’s coverage treated the launch as Apple’s first serious move into downloadable video distribution, with Disney/ABC content playing a major role in the announcement. Source: Wired, October 2005 video iPod coverage. This mattered because the iPod was no longer only about music. It was becoming a portable media platform. The screen grew in importance. The store expanded beyond songs. The living-room and pocket worlds began to touch. Apple did not yet have the iPhone, but it was learning how a handheld device, content store, media catalog, and screen could become one commercial system. The iPod family was therefore not a detour from the smartphone. It was a laboratory. Retail, accessories, and the object on the counter The iPod also arrived at the same time Apple was learning how to sell directly to consumers.

Page 132

The first Apple Stores opened in 2001, the same year as the iPod. This matters because a pocket device benefits from being touched before being bought. A computer can be compared through specifications and software needs; a small personal object has to feel right. The iPod’s weight, wheel, brightness, finish, packaging, earbuds, and dock accessories were all easier to understand in person. Apple’s retail stores gave the company a stage smaller than Macworld but larger than a shelf in someone else’s electronics aisle. They let Apple control the explanation. Carrier stores would later control much of the phone-buying experience. Before the iPhone, that was one of the mobile industry’s deepest structural facts. Apple learned retail in a different category first. It learned how to display a device, how to let users handle it, how to bundle services around it, how to turn packaging and store environment into part of the brand, and how to make a technical object feel approachable. When Apple later sold the iPhone through its own stores as well as through carrier arrangements, it was not starting from scratch. The iPod had brought millions of non-Mac customers into Apple’s retail world. The accessory ecosystem was another rehearsal. Cases, docks, speakers, car adapters, chargers, FM transmitters, armbands, engraving, cables, headphones, and home stereos appeared around the iPod. Some were Apple’s. Many were not. The device became valuable enough that other companies built businesses around it. Wired later noted Apple’s partnerships and add-on ecosystem as part of how the iPod maintained its market position, and Apple’s own 2022 retrospective treated the iPod not as a single device but as a lineage whose music experience eventually spread across iPhone, Watch, iPad, Mac, AirPods, HomePod mini, and Apple Music. Sources: Wired iPod retrospective; Apple Newsroom, "The music lives on," May 2022. This was important because accessories make a device more physical in daily life. A dock on the desk changes the ritual. Speakers in the kitchen change the location. A car adapter changes the commute. An armband changes exercise. A case changes fashion. An engraved back turns a gadget into a gift. A device with accessories becomes less like a standalone object and more like a system of habits. The smartphone would later have an even larger accessory economy: cases, chargers, docks, screen protectors, mounts, headphones, watch pairings, car systems, repair tools, and entire retail walls of small attachments. The iPod helped establish Apple’s version of that world. It taught the company that once people carried a beloved pocket device, they would pay to protect it, display it, personalize it, connect it, and make it fit into more rooms and routines. The iPod also taught Apple the power of premium margins in a mass device. The company did not try to win the MP3-player market by being cheapest. It won by being clearer, better

integrated, and more desirable, then expanded downward with cheaper models without making the brand feel cheap. That strategy would later reappear in the iPhone: high-end first, expanding over time, with older models, carrier subsidies, installments, trade-ins, and regional strategies doing the work of broadening access. The halo The iPod changed Apple’s relationship with people who did not think of themselves as Apple people. That change had a name: the halo effect. The idea was that iPod users, having bought and loved an Apple product, might become more open to buying Macs. In 2004, Wired reported Wall Street excitement around the possibility that iPod word-of-mouth was driving Mac interest, citing a Piper Jaffray survey suggesting that a portion of iPod users had switched or were planning to switch from PC to Mac, though the article also treated the numbers with caution. Source: Wired, "Hallowed Halo?" November 2004. The precise size of the halo is less important than the fact that the iPod altered Apple’s public identity. The company was no longer only the maker of the Mac, the alternative computer for designers, students, loyalists, and people who disliked Windows. It was the maker of the object everyone seemed to want. That changed the psychological weather around Apple. A person who would never buy a Mac could buy an iPod. A Windows user could install iTunes. A teenager could want Apple for music rather than computing. A parent could enter an Apple Store for a gift rather than a computer. A carmaker, speaker company, hotel, airline, gym, or retailer could build around iPod compatibility. The halo was not only about Mac sales. It was about trust. The smartphone would require enormous trust. A phone is not a music player. It is more intimate, more necessary, more regulated, more expensive in monthly cost, more tied to identity, more expected to work in emergencies, and more deeply connected to the outside world. Apple could not have entered that category as easily if the public had known it only as a niche computer maker. The iPod made Apple a mass consumer brand again. It also changed Apple internally. A company that ships a few million computers a year operates differently from a company that ships tens of millions of small devices. The iPod forced Apple to manage high-volume consumer electronics, component sourcing, retail demand, global distribution, color variations, seasonal gift cycles, accessory partners, music- label relationships, Windows software, and rapid model iteration. These were not side lessons. They were the organizational skills a future phone would need.

By April 2007, Apple had sold its 100 millionth iPod, less than six years after introduction, and the iTunes Store had sold billions of songs. Wired’s contemporaneous coverage of the 100-million milestone compared the iPod’s scale with the Walkman lineage and emphasized the tight connection between iPod sales and iTunes music purchases. Source: Wired, "100M iPods Sold," April 2007. That timing is almost too neat. In January 2007, Apple announced the iPhone. In April, it announced the 100 millionth iPod. The old pocket device was at its peak just as the new one was preparing to cannibalize it. The iPod had made Apple powerful enough to risk replacing itself. What the phone industry saw The phone industry saw the iPod, too. It had to. By the mid-2000s, music was one of the most obvious features phones wanted to absorb. Feature phones already had ringtones, limited music playback, memory cards, cameras, games, and carrier content stores. Sony Ericsson had Walkman-branded phones. Nokia had music devices. Motorola, Samsung, LG, and others saw that if the phone was always with the user, it could become the music player. The logic was inescapable. Why carry an iPod and a phone if the phone could play songs? This was not theoretical. It was the same pressure that had shaped earlier convergence devices. Why carry a Palm and a phone? Why carry a pager and a phone? Why carry a camera and a phone? Why carry a laptop and a communicator? The phone was the object already required by the network and by social life. Every other pocket device eventually had to answer the phone’s challenge. For Apple, this created an existential anxiety around the iPod. The iPod was the company’s great growth engine and cultural symbol, but mobile phones were becoming the universal pocket platform. If phones became good music players, the iPod could become another standalone device swallowed by convergence. Apple had already benefited from making a dedicated music player better than clumsy competitors. But the phone did not have to be a perfect music player to threaten it. It only had to be good enough for millions of users who did not want two devices. This was the background behind the Motorola ROKR, the iTunes phone that will take center stage in the next chapter. The ROKR was an attempt to let Apple’s music system enter the phone world without Apple having to build the phone itself. In theory, that could have protected the iPod and extended iTunes. In practice, it exposed the limits of grafting Apple’s software and music strategy onto a carrier-controlled handset designed by someone else.

The iPod had taught Apple to control the whole experience: device, software, sync, store, brand, packaging, updates, and retail. The phone industry was built differently. Carriers controlled distribution and features. Handset makers controlled hardware. Music labels controlled content. Users controlled desire. A compromised iTunes phone would not be enough if the phone was going to become the next great pocket computer. The iPod therefore did two contradictory things. It made Apple rich and confident enough to enter phones. It also made the danger of not entering phones impossible to ignore. The private world before the connected world The iPod was not connected in the way smartphones would be connected. That was part of its character. A BlackBerry was alive with incoming messages. A phone was alive with calls and texts. An i-mode handset was alive with services and packet data. The iPod was quiet unless the user chose otherwise. It did not interrupt. It did not demand replies. It did not know where it was. It did not ask for a data plan. It did not turn attention outward toward a feed, inbox, or browser. It turned attention inward toward a chosen library. That makes the iPod feel almost innocent in retrospect. But it was not small in cultural consequence. It changed the expectation of what a pocket device could hold. Not only numbers and dates. Not only messages. Not only calls. A pocket device could hold mood. It could hold memory. It could hold identity in aesthetic form. It could be the object a person reached for not because someone else needed them, but because they wanted to shape the feeling of their own moment. This is why the iPod belongs in the smartphone’s genealogy. The smartphone was not only a better phone. It was also a better private media device, a better organizer, a better camera, a better browser, a better messaging terminal, and eventually a better store. The iPhone’s first public framing - an iPod, a phone, and an internet communicator - worked because the iPod part was already emotionally complete. Apple did not have to explain why a pocket music player mattered. It had already spent six years proving it. The iPod also prepared users for the idea that a pocket device could be updated, synced, accessorized, personalized, and tied to a software library. It taught them to plug in. It taught them to carry white earbuds. It taught them that a scrollable list could hold abundance. It taught them that Apple could make a small device worth a premium. It taught them that the computer and the pocket could be partners. It taught Apple that the hand, the library, the store, and the brand had to be designed together.

The lesson Apple carried forward The iPod’s lessons were not abstract, and they were not all about music. Apple learned that a pocket device needs one obvious sentence. “1,000 songs in your pocket.” Later: “an iPod, a phone, and an internet communicator.” The sentence does not replace engineering. It disciplines it. If the product cannot be explained simply, the experience probably is not simple enough. Apple learned that synchronization could be emotional. A cable was not only a cable. It was the connection between the large library and the pocket self. Later, contacts, calendars, photos, apps, backups, and cloud services would all become part of that same emotional problem: how does the thing in the pocket remain continuous with the rest of a person’s life? Apple learned that content and hardware amplify each other. The iPod made iTunes more useful; iTunes made the iPod more valuable; the store made both feel legitimate; Windows support made the system larger; accessories made it more present in rooms, cars, and bodies. Apple learned that small devices need dedicated interfaces. The scroll wheel was not a miniature desktop metaphor. It was a pocket-native answer to a long-list problem. The iPhone’s multi-touch interface would later be a pocket-native answer to a different problem: how to make a general-purpose software device feel direct without a physical keyboard. Apple learned how to manage desire. The iPod was not merely a specification winner. It was a social object, a gift, a fashion marker, a private world, a piece of public identity. The iPhone would have to become all of those things while also being a phone. And perhaps most importantly, Apple learned that control mattered. Control of hardware, control of software, control of the store, control of the industrial design, control of the packaging, control of the retail story, control of the update path, control of the user’s first five minutes with the device. The phone industry would resist that kind of control because carriers were used to owning the customer relationship. But the iPod had shown Apple what

a vertically choreographed pocket experience could feel like. Once the company had learned that, it was unlikely to accept being merely a software supplier to someone else’s handset. The iPod did not make the iPhone inevitable. Nothing did. But it made the iPhone imaginable inside Apple in a different way. Without the iPod, Apple might still have tried to build a phone, but it would not have had the same muscles: the small-device team, the supply-chain knowledge, the content-store experience, the retail confidence, the accessory ecosystem, the public trust, the brand momentum, or the lived understanding that people could fall in love with an object small enough to vanish into a pocket. The feature-phone chapter ended with the phone becoming everywhere. The iPod chapter ends with Apple learning how to make everywhere feel intimate. The next question was unavoidable. If the phone was going to eat the music player, would Apple let someone else hold the fork?
